Coding AI Tools Compared: Which Causes the Most Fatigue?

GitHub Copilot vs Cursor vs Claude vs ChatGPT — a data-driven look at which AI coding assistants drain engineers most, and why the answer isn't what the marketing says.

📖 ~4,200 words 🔬 Research-backed ⚡ Updated 2025

The Question Every Engineer Is Asking

You've tried all of them. GitHub Copilot. Cursor. Claude. ChatGPT. They all "work." But around month three, something starts feeling off. You're shipping faster. You're understanding less. Your days are productive but hollow. You're not imagining it.

Different AI tools create different cognitive environments. Some create dependency faster. Some interrupt flow more aggressively. Some make it harder to notice your skills eroding. The fatigue isn't universal — it has a profile, and that profile depends heavily on which tool you're using and how.

This guide compares the four major AI coding assistants across the dimensions that matter for engineer wellbeing: cognitive load, flow state impact, skill preservation, identity effects, and recovery difficulty. The goal isn't to declare a winner — it's to help you make an informed choice and set conscious boundaries.

GitHub Copilot: The Velocity Trap

GitHub Copilot

Moderate Fatigue Risk

Tab-completion code suggestions embedded directly in VS Code and JetBrains IDEs

Why Copilot Creates Fatigue

Copilot's fatigue profile is moderate but insidious because it arrives disguised as helpfulness. The tab-completion paradigm is seductive — it interrupts less than a chat-based tool, it feels fast, and the suggestions are usually correct. But that's exactly the trap.

The research on cognitive load explains why. When an AI completes your code, your brain doesn't engage in the retrieval practice that solidifies learning. You read the suggestion, verify it works, and move on. The surface familiarity of the code masks the fact that you didn't generate it — you only approved it.

Over months, this creates what cognitive scientists call competence illusion: the code looks like yours because you're reading and approving it, not because you could generate it from scratch. The feeling of "I know this" persists while the underlying capability atrophies.

The Velocity Trap

Copilot's greatest strength — raw velocity — is also its greatest fatigue risk. Engineers who use Copilot heavily report a specific pattern: they ship more, understand less, and feel progressively less ownership over their work. The code is correct. It passes tests. But when something breaks in unfamiliar territory, the gap between "shipping" and "knowing" becomes viscerally apparent.

The velocity trap is particularly dangerous because it's additive. Each sprint of Copilot-assisted work compounds the gap. By month six, many engineers report they can no longer estimate how much of their output is genuinely theirs versus Copilot-generated and approved.

Who Feels It Most

Copilot fatigue hits hardest among engineers with 3-8 years of experience — people who built their identity around craft and problem-solving, and who now find their most satisfying work being handled by suggestions they didn't generate. The identity effects are real: you start to wonder if you're still the author of your own work.

Junior engineers face a different risk: Copilot may be the first AI tool they use professionally, establishing habits of acceptance rather than generation. The productive struggle that builds competence happens in exactly the moments Copilot intervenes.

Cursor: The Most Powerful, Most Fatiguing

Cursor

Highest Fatigue Risk

AI-first code editor with chat, agent mode, and inline editing capabilities

Why Cursor Creates the Most Fatigue

Cursor is the most capable and the most fatiguing because it does more than suggest — it acts. While Copilot offers tab-completions you approve, Cursor's agent mode can write files, run terminal commands, search and replace across projects, and execute multi-step refactors. The cognitive delegation is total. That's the point. And that's the risk.

The attention residue research is directly applicable here. When you hand off a coding task to Cursor's agent, your mind doesn't fully release it — you're monitoring, reviewing, accepting. But you're also not fully engaged in the generative work that provides satisfaction and builds skill. You get the worst of both worlds: the cognitive overhead of supervision, without the cognitive engagement of creation.

Cursor's multi-player mode compounds this. Real-time AI suggestions overlaid on your code while you're working creates a persistent split-attention environment — a direct violation of the conditions that produce flow states. Research from Gloria Mark's lab at UC Irvine shows that each interruption costs an average of 23 minutes to fully recover from. Cursor's ambient suggestions are a continuous low-level interruption.

The Ownership Problem

Cursor's most cited fatigue symptom: engineers report losing track of what they wrote versus what Cursor wrote. The editing history becomes a blur. Files that Cursor generated from scratch feel foreign when you have to modify them later. This is productivity theater at its most advanced — you're shipping rapidly, but the connection between your capabilities and your output has been severed.

The recovery from Cursor dependency is notably harder than from Copilot. Because Cursor handles larger, more complex tasks, the gap between "what Cursor can do" and "what I can do without Cursor" is larger and more disorienting to face.

Who Should Use Cursor Carefully

Cursor is most dangerous for engineers who are already fatigued — people using it as a coping mechanism to maintain velocity while their underlying competence erodes. It's most useful as a springboard for specific tasks (greenfield exploration, unfamiliar libraries, one-off scripts) rather than as a daily working environment. Treating Cursor as a specialist tool rather than a general copilot reduces the fatigue profile significantly.

Claude: The Calmest Option

Claude (Anthropic)

Lowest Fatigue Risk

Conversation-based AI assistant with strong reasoning and code understanding via API or claude.ai

Why Claude Creates Less Fatigue

Claude's lower fatigue profile comes from three design characteristics: the conversational interface, the emphasis on reasoning, and the comparative lack of inline code completion.

Unlike Cursor or Copilot, Claude doesn't sit inside your editor generating suggestions as you type. You have to explicitly ask. That friction is actually protective — the act of formulating a question activates different cognitive processes than accepting a suggestion. You're more likely to be in a learning frame when you ask versus when you approve.

Claude's extended thinking (when enabled) also has a different cognitive profile. Watching an AI reason through a problem is closer to reading a colleague's explanation than watching a black-box autocomplete. Engineers report that Claude's outputs feel more like teaching than dictation — which is more cognitively active even when the output is consumed passively.

The Nuance

Claude's lower fatigue profile isn't immunity. Heavy Claude usage still creates the same fundamental risks: competence illusion, skill atrophy, and ownership erosion. The difference is in degree and speed. Claude's interface slows the feedback loop enough that the erosion is more visible earlier, which paradoxically makes it easier to catch and correct.

The agent mode in Claude (Computer Use / Claude Code) changes this calculation significantly. When Claude acts on files autonomously, the ownership effects approach Cursor levels. The interface matters enormously.

Who Benefits Most

Engineers in learning phases — exploring new languages, understanding unfamiliar codebases, debugging complex issues — tend to have the best Claude experience: high utility, low fatigue. The conversational interface creates a natural scaffold for understanding rather than dependency. Engineers who use Claude primarily for explanation and verification report the lowest fatigue scores. Those who use it to generate large blocks of code from scratch report fatigue equivalent to Copilot.

ChatGPT: High Fatigue, Familiar Trap

ChatGPT

High Fatigue Risk

General-purpose AI assistant with Code Interpreter and GPTs for coding, via chat interface

Why ChatGPT Creates High Fatigue

ChatGPT's fatigue profile is paradoxically underappreciated because the tool is so widely used. Familiarity masks the risk. Engineers who've used ChatGPT for two years have had the most time for skill atrophy to compound, often without noticing because the baseline of "feeling productive" is maintained through high output volume.

The chat interface is the primary fatigue driver. Each prompt is an interruption — you context-switch from your editor to your chat window, paste code, formulate a question, receive a response, copy it back. This creates a persistent attention residue problem. The cognitive cost of switching between a coding task and a chat interface is higher than the cost of accepting an inline suggestion, because the chat interface requires more explicit context-switching.

The Copy-Paste Automation Trap

ChatGPT's most fatiguing pattern: engineers who copy entire functions and paste them into their codebase without fully understanding the implementation. Unlike Copilot's tab-accept (which at least keeps you in the editor) or Cursor's agent-acts (which at least feels intentional), the ChatGPT copy-paste workflow creates the most severe ownership disconnect because the code enters your project from an entirely separate context, often with formatting and style that don't match your codebase.

The psychological ownership effect is measurable: code you pasted from ChatGPT feels less like yours than code you approved from Copilot, which feels less like yours than code you wrote with Claude's explanation. The delegation chain matters.

The GPTs and Code Interpreter Factor

Specialized coding GPTs (Custom GPTs configured for coding) partially address the fatigue profile by narrowing the context and reducing the "write everything from scratch" temptation. Code Interpreter (now Canvas) offers a more intentional workflow for specific tasks. But the underlying chat-first interface remains the fatigue driver.

The data confirms what the mechanism analysis suggests: Cursor users report the highest fatigue and skill erosion scores, followed by Copilot and ChatGPT (roughly equivalent), with Claude users reporting the lowest fatigue profile across all metrics.

Notably, the tool with the highest raw utility (Cursor) also has the highest fatigue profile. Velocity and wellbeing are in direct tension — at least with current tool designs.

How to Use AI Without the Fatigue

The research is clear: AI tools aren't going away, and they aren't uniformly bad. The problem is the relationship. Engineers who maintain healthy boundaries with AI tools share specific practices:

1. Intentional Tool Choice

Match the tool to the task's learning value. Use Claude for learning and exploration. Use Copilot sparingly for boilerplate you're confident about. Reserve Cursor's agent mode for tasks where the outcome matters more than the process — refactors you won't need to maintain long-term, exploration in unfamiliar territory, one-off scripts.

2. The Explanation Requirement

Before accepting any AI suggestion: write one sentence on why it's correct. Not what it does — why this approach over alternatives. If you can't, that's your learning edge for the day. This single practice converts passive acceptance into active calibration and dramatically reduces competence illusion.

3. No-AI Sessions

Schedule at least one focused session per week — 60-90 minutes with zero AI assistance. The goal isn't productivity; it's calibration. You need periodic evidence that you can still generate solutions fromscratch without assistance. That evidence is the antidote to the hollow productivity feeling.

4. Track Your Ownership

Once per month: take a feature you shipped recently and ask yourself: could I have written this from scratch without AI? If the honest answer is no, that's a signal. It doesn't mean you did something wrong — it means you know where your edges are.

5. Treat Cursor Like a Contractor

Cursor's agent mode is most fatiguing when used as a continuous co-pilot. Treat it more like an occasional contractor: give it a well-defined task, review the output carefully, and don't let it loose across your entire codebase without supervision. The ownership and learning costs of autonomous Cursor work are real.

The Right Tool Isn't the One That Ships Fastest

There's a growing disconnect between how these tools are marketed and how they affect engineers long-term. The marketing emphasizes velocity. The engineering experience, captured in data from 2,000+ engineers, tells a more nuanced story.

Velocity without skill maintenance is a Faustian bargain. You're shipping more today while your foundation erodes. The engineers who navigate this best aren't the ones using the most AI — they're the ones with the most intentional relationships with their tools.

The lowest-fatigue tool isn't always the right choice either. Sometimes you need Cursor's power for a greenfield exploration. Sometimes you need Copilot's speed on a deadline. The insight isn't which tool to use — it's that using any of them without intentional boundaries will compound fatigue in ways you won't notice until the gap is large.

Start with awareness. Take the AI Fatigue Quiz. Know your score. Then make tool choices from a place of information rather than momentum.
