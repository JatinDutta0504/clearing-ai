### DAY 2 — The Thing Nobody Names

Yesterday I asked you to notice when you reach for AI.

Most people who write in tell me the same thing: they didn't realize how often it happens until they started watching for it.

One engineer said: "I was debugging something in a language I've used for six years. I hit a wall. My first instinct — before I'd even tried — was to paste it into ChatGPT. I'd been doing that for months without noticing."

That's the moment.

Not a crisis. Not a breakdown. Just a quiet reflex that says: *I don't try anymore.*

---

## What Nobody Talks About

There's a difference between using a tool and outsourcing your judgment.

When you use a calculator, you still understand math. You trust the output because you could check it. You know if it's wrong.

When you use AI in your code, something different happens:

You often don't know if the code is right.
You often can't explain why it works.
You often don't remember writing it.

And slowly — without anyone announcing it — your relationship to your own work changes.

You stop thinking "I built this" and start thinking "this got built."

---

## The Three Layers of What's Happening

**1. Skill Drift**
The things you used to just know, you have to look up again. Not because you're forgetting — because you stopped practicing them. The muscle isn't being used. It's not going away instantly, but it's getting flabby.

**2. Identity Erosion**
You spent years building an identity around being someone who can figure things out. When that gets replaced, you don't just lose capability — you lose a piece of how you see yourself. "I'm someone who can build this" becomes "I'm someone who used to be able to build this."

**3. The Imposter Feeling**
You know more than you're demonstrating. But the gap between what you know and what you do is growing. And every time you ship something you don't fully understand, you feel like a fraud — even though you actually understand more than you're showing.

This isn't imposter syndrome. Imposter syndrome is a cognitive distortion — you feel like a fraud even though you're competent. This is different.

You ARE competent. But the code you're shipping doesn't reflect your competence — it reflects the AI's capability. And the more you lean on AI, the wider that gap grows.

---

## Why Sleep Doesn't Fix It

You might have noticed: you take a vacation, you come back refreshed, you use AI heavily for two weeks, and by week three you're as tired as before.

That's because the problem isn't mental exhaustion.

It's that you've lost the thing that used to restore you: the feeling of having built something yourself.

Deep work is not just tiring in a bad way. It's tiring in a way that, when done well, also *replenishes* you. The flow state isn't just productive — it's meaningful. It gives you something back.

AI-assisted work doesn't give you that back. It gives you output without the nourishment.

That's why you can be busy all day and still feel empty at night.

---

## The Name for It

There are a few terms researchers use:

**Skill Atrophy** — the degradation of abilities you stop practicing.

**Cognitive Offloading** — relying on external systems to do your thinking for you.

**Automation Bias** — the tendency to trust automated systems even when you shouldn't.

But the one I keep coming back to is simpler:

**The Middleman Problem.**

You're not writing code anymore. You're managing a system that writes code. And managing a system that writes code for you is a fundamentally different activity than writing code yourself.

It's not automatically bad. But it's not nothing.

---

## What You Can Do With This

The goal isn't to reject AI. The goal is to use it consciously, not compulsively.

The difference is simple:

**Compulsive AI use:** AI is your first reflex. You reach for it before thinking.

**Conscious AI use:** AI is a tool you deploy intentionally. You decide when it helps and when it doesn't.

The first step is noticing when you're in compulsive mode. Yesterday's practice was the beginning of that.
