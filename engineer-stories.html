Full-stack · 5 yearsTier 2 — Some Fatigue
"

The worst part wasn't the fatigue. It was that I stopped being curious. I'd used to read tech blogs at lunch, think about architectural decisions on walks, pick up a new language on weekends for fun. Then AI could answer my questions instantly, and I stopped asking. My curiosity didn't fade — it got replaced. Every question I would have looked up, I asked the AI. Instant answer, instant closure. No gap to sit in, no moment of not-knowing that made the answer worth finding. The quiz helped me see this pattern. I scored 8. Now I keep one 'stupid question' per week — something I won't ask AI, something I have to figure out myself. Feels embarrassing. Also feels like me again.

🧠"I thought I was lazy. Turns out I just had no practice staying in the not-knowing."

Junior Engineer · 1.5 yearsTier 3 — Real Fatigue
"

I got my first dev job 18 months ago. I learned to code with AI tools from the start — when something broke, AI explained it. When I didn't understand something, AI clarified it. Then my tech lead asked me to whiteboard the system we'd been building. I opened my mouth and nothing came out. I could navigate the codebase. I could explain individual pieces. I could not explain the whole. I scored 11 on the quiz. The 'junior engineers' page hit me like a truck. The skills I was supposed to be building were being built in the AI, not in me. I didn't have a mental model of the system — I had a route map through it. I'm now doing 'debug Sunday' — three hours with no AI, just me and a broken feature. It's humbling. It's also the only time I feel like I'm actually learning anything.

📉"Productive from the outside. Hollow from the inside. That gap is the real problem."

DevOps Engineer · 4 yearsTier 1 — Holding Up
"

I'm one of those annoying people who reads documentation end-to-end before touching a new tool. Yes, it takes longer. No, I don't regret it. When AI coding assistants became mandatory at my company, I noticed the gap immediately: people who reached for AI first had no mental model of what the code was doing. I kept reading the docs. I kept building the map. Scored 3 on the quiz. My colleagues score higher — some much higher. The thing I've noticed: the people who are in trouble are the ones who can't calibrate their own uncertainty anymore. They can't tell when something is actually hard or when they just haven't thought it through. AI removes the calibration signal. My practice now: every time I use AI to solve something, I then try to solve it without AI. Not to prove a point. To keep the calibration signal alive.

📊"AI removes the calibration signal. You can't calibrate your own uncertainty when AI is always there."
