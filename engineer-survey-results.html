The survey was distributed via the clearing-ai.com quiz funnel, r/cscareerquestions, Hacker News, and four developer newsletters. Respondents self-selected; data is weighted to reflect the working software engineer population by experience tier.

Finding 01

63% of engineers feel like "middlemen" — shipping code they don't own

The authorship crisis is structural, not psychological

Nearly two-thirds of surveyed engineers reported a persistent sense that the code they ship is no longer fully theirs. Not imposter syndrome — a functional, measurable shift in the authorship experience. The code ships. It works. They can't fully explain why.

When asked to compare their relationship with AI-assisted code versus code written from scratch, 63% reported lower sense of ownership, 58% reported lower sense of having earned the solution, and 47% reported difficulty explaining their own code to teammates without AI mediation.

"I used to read code and feel like I understood it at the cellular level. Now I read AI-generated code and understand it at the functional level — but there's a gap. Like reading a book versus reading a summary of a book. I can't always tell you why it works, only that it does." — Senior IC, 9 years experience, Bay Area
💡

Why this matters: This is not imposter syndrome. Imposter syndrome is a cognitive distortion — engineers believing they're less capable than they are. This is a functional change: the relationship between engineer and code has genuinely shifted. The code being shipped genuinely is less entirely theirs than it was two years ago. That's not a perception problem. It's a structural problem.

The finding held across experience levels, but was most pronounced among engineers with 5–12 years of experience (67%) — those who built their identity around craft mastery before AI tools became widespread. Junior engineers (0–3 years) reported lower rates (44%) — many never developed the pre-AI authorship relationship that senior engineers are mourning.

Finding 02

58% report measurable skill decline — and most don't notice until it's severe

The competence illusion is the most dangerous pattern in AI-assisted development

When asked whether their core technical skills had changed since integrating AI coding tools, 58% of engineers reported measurable decline in at least one foundational skill. The most affected areas:

The most dangerous finding: 41% of those reporting skill decline said they only realized the decline when they tried to do something without AI — suggesting that AI tools aren't just causing skill atrophy, they're actively hiding it via the competence illusion. When AI handles the parts you're weakest at, you mistake overall capability for retained skill.

Notably, 23% of engineers reported a perceived increase in their skills — almost exclusively in areas like "prompt engineering," "AI workflow design," and "code architecture at the component level." But most of this group also reported decline in foundational skills, suggesting they trade one type of capability for another.

"I didn't notice until my laptop died and I had to write a sorting function by hand for a technical interview prep session. I used to be able to do this in my sleep. I couldn't. And I couldn't tell if I was nervous or if the skill was actually gone." — Mid-level IC, 5 years, remote (EU)
Finding 03

71% take the quiz as a "test" — but only 23% act on the results within a week

Awareness without action is just another form of anxiety

We asked quiz-takers why they took the AI Fatigue Quiz. 71% said they were primarily curious whether what they were experiencing had a name — they suspected it did, but hadn't articulated it. This "test-taking" motivation was the single largest segment.

Motivation Share Notes
"Does this have a name?" — curiosity 71% Wanted to validate what they were feeling
"I think I have it" — confirmation 54% Already suspected AI fatigue, wanted data
"Just curious" — passing time 41% No strong expectation of outcome
"How bad is it?" — severity check 38% Already experiencing symptoms, wanted to know stage
"For a friend" — other-referral 29% Recognizing symptoms in someone else
Took action within 7 days 23% Most common action: shared with one person / changed one habit
📊

The awareness-action gap: The quiz creates naming clarity — 91% of takers said "knowing it has a name helped." But naming without action plan = sophisticated anxiety. The 23% who took action within a week did one of three things: talked to their manager, blocked one no-AI day per week, or started a daily reflection practice.

Finding 04

44% have considered leaving the industry — 18% actively planning an exit

The retention crisis hasn't peaked yet

Perhaps the most consequential finding: 44% of surveyed engineers reported actively considering leaving software engineering as a career. Not because they don't love building — but because the conditions of building have changed in ways they find untenable.

When asked specifically what they would miss if they left, the top responses were: "building things that work" (78%), "solving hard problems" (67%), "being surrounded by smart people" (61%), and "the identity of being an engineer" (44%). These are people who love the substance of the work but find the current conditions damaging.

"I don't want to leave. I want the job I fell in love with back. But that job doesn't seem to exist anymore. I don't know if it's the tools or the industry or me." — Staff Engineer, 12 years, distributed team
🚨

For managers and CTOs: This is a retention signal, not a resignation inevitability. The 44% considering leaving aren't doing so because they want out — they're doing so because the conditions feel unrecoverable. Our data shows the top 3 retention factors are: protected no-AI deep work time, visible leadership on the AI fatigue issue, and quarterly skill calibration conversations with their manager.

Finding 05

What actually helps: the evidence from engineers who recovered

Not productivity hacks — structural changes in how you relate to AI tools

We asked the 19% of respondents who reported significant improvement in their AI fatigue over the past 6 months what changed. Their answers were remarkably consistent — not around tool choice, but around relationship to tools.

Practice Who found it helpful Why it works
No-AI coding blocks (90 min, 1-2x/week) 67% Preserves skill, rebuilds confidence, re-anchors identity
Explanation Requirement (explain AI code aloud before accepting) 71% Restores cognitive engagement, surfaces gaps, builds ownership
Daily reflection journal (5 min EOD) 44% Creates cognitive closure, processes accumulated overwhelm
Sunday planning without AI (30 min) 52% Breaks Sunday dread cycle, reclaims agency for the week
Quarterly skill calibration (no-AI side project) 38% Objective self-assessment, counters competence illusion
Manager conversation about AI expectations 29% Reduces pressure to perform with AI, restores autonomy
🌱

Most underrated recovery practice: The Explanation Requirement — before accepting any AI-generated code, explain it out loud as if teaching a colleague. If you can't explain it, you don't own it. 71% of recovered engineers listed this as their single highest-impact change. It takes 2 minutes. It restores cognitive engagement, surfaces skill gaps, and rebuilds authorship — all at once.

The 4–8 year engineers are the canary in the coal mine. Highest authorship loss (67%), highest skill decline reports (63%), highest exit consideration (51%). They've been in the industry long enough to know what they've lost, but not long enough to have a fully stable identity outside of "being a good engineer." This is also the talent segment most likely to be poached by startups and most likely to leave entirely.

Finding 07

The compounding effect: 77% experience 3+ symptoms simultaneously

AI fatigue isn't a single problem. It's a compounding system.

When we mapped the co-occurrence of AI fatigue symptoms, we found that single-symptom AI fatigue is rare. 77% of respondents reporting "significant fatigue" had at least three co-occurring symptoms — and the compounding effect made each worse than it would be in isolation.

77%
3+ symptoms at once
43%
5+ symptoms at once
19%
All 6 core symptoms

The most common compounding cluster: authorship loss + skill anxiety + Sunday dread. These three feed each other in a loop: you don't own the code → you worry the skills are eroding → Sunday you dread another week of producing code you don't own. Breaking any one link helps. Breaking the authorship link first has the most leverage.

"It's not one thing. It's that I feel weird about the code AND I can't explain it to myself AND I dread Sundays AND I feel like I'm falling behind AND I don't know what 'senior' even means anymore. It's not burnout where you just need to rest. It's all of it, all the time." — Mid-level IC, 6 years, Series B startup
Conclusion

What this data means — and what we can do about it

This is a structural problem. It requires structural answers.

The dominant narrative around AI and software engineers is about adaptation: learn new tools, upskill, embrace change. Our data suggests that framing misses the actual problem. The engineers experiencing AI fatigue aren't Luddites — they're experiencing a genuine restructuring of their relationship with their craft.

The 63% who feel like middlemen aren't wrong. They are shipping code they don't fully own. The 58% with measurable skill decline aren't imagining it. The atrophy is real. The 44% considering leaving aren't being dramatic. They're responding rationally to conditions that feel unrecoverable without structural change.

What individual engineers can do

Recovery isn't about using less AI — it's about maintaining cognitive agency in your relationship with AI tools. The Explanation Requirement, no-AI coding blocks, and regular skill calibration aren't anti-AI practices. They're practices that keep you in the loop, not outsourced from it.

What managers and organizations can do

The highest-leverage organizational changes are: establishing explicit norms around when AI is and isn't used in code review, protecting uninterrupted deep work time, having direct conversations with engineers about AI fatigue (normalizing it), and building quarterly skill calibration into the rhythm of the team. None of these cost money. They require leadership attention.

What this survey doesn't answer

We don't know yet whether these patterns are permanent or reversible at scale. We don't know whether the skill atrophy is recoverable for most engineers or whether it represents a permanent ratchet. We don't have longitudinal data on whether AI fatigue compounds into full burnout or plateaus. These are the questions we're tracking over time — and we'll publish updates as the data comes in.
