📐 How We Ran This Survey

In January–February 2025, The Clearing distributed an anonymous 22-question survey to engineers who took our AI Fatigue Quiz on clearing-ai.com and opted in to follow-up research. No company affiliations were collected. Responses came from 47 countries, with concentration in the US (41%), UK (14%), Germany (8%), Canada (7%), and India (6%).

Respondents were segmented by role (frontend/backend/ML/ops/fullstack/lead/other), seniority (0–2 / 3–5 / 6–10 / 10+ years), AI tool usage frequency (daily / several times a week / occasional), and company type (startup / mid-market / enterprise / freelance).

The Code Ownership Crisis

The most striking finding: nearly two-thirds of engineers no longer feel like they wrote the code they ship. This isn't imposter syndrome — it's a structural change in what "coding" means.

How Engineers Describe Their Relationship to Code

"I write code with AI — it feels collaborative" 34%
"I feel like a middleman directing AI output" 41%
"I barely code anymore — mostly review and approve" 22%
"I still feel full authorship over what I ship" 18%

Note: Percentages do not sum to 100 — respondents could select multiple answers or none.

Senior Engineers Feel It Most

Engineers with 8+ years of experience are 2.3x more likely to report "middleman" feelings than those with under 3 years. The craft identity hit is proportional to what you've built before AI.

Frontend Engineers Feel It Fastest

Frontend/UI engineers reported the sharpest ownership decline — often within 4–6 months of daily AI use. The high volume of small, repetitive tasks AI handles well accelerated the effect.

ML Engineers Are an Exception

Engineers focused on ML/AI model work reported the highest continued ownership feelings. Building the AI that others use seems to preserve a different kind of authorship.

"I used to be proud of my variable names. Now I just accept whatever ChatGPT generates and move on. I don't even read half of it."

— Senior backend engineer, 9 years experience, survey respondent

The Skill Atrophy Signal

58% of engineers report a measurable decline in at least one core coding skill. The most commonly cited: debugging, algorithmic design, and reading unfamiliar codebases.

Skill Area Reported Decline Severity Primary Cause Cited
Debugging from scratch 67% High AI fixes errors before I trace them
Reading unfamiliar code 61% High Skip reading, go straight to AI explanation
Algorithmic problem design 54% Moderate AI solves the design, I implement
Writing tests 49% Moderate AI generates, I accept — rarely verify
System design thinking 43% Moderate Skip architecture discussions, ask AI
Code review quality 38% Mild Trust AI caught it, review surface-level
Writing documentation 29% Mild AI handles it adequately

The Junior Engineer Amplification

Engineers with under 3 years of experience are 41% more likely to report no skill decline — but 3x more likely to report never having developed certain skills in the first place. The atrophy signal is hiding a more fundamental failure to build the skill.

The 90-Day Rule

Among engineers who reported skill decline, 78% noticed the first signs within 90 days of starting daily AI-assisted coding. The effect is fast and often unnoticed until it's advanced.

The Counter-Intuitive Finding

Engineers who maintain "no-AI" coding blocks (at least 2 hours per week) reported 62% less skill decline than those who use AI continuously throughout the workday.

"I was a debugging machine after 8 years. I could find anything in any codebase. Now I catch myself asking AI to explain my own code back to me."

— Staff engineer, 11 years, survey respondent

The Identity Shift: From Craftsman to Operator

The data reveals a profound shift in how engineers describe their professional identity. The "craftsman" frame is cracking under the weight of AI-mediated work.

How Engineers Describe Their Work Now

"I'm a software engineer — I build things" 31%
"I mostly prompt and approve AI output" 38%
"I coordinate AI tools more than I code" 44%
"I'm not sure what my job is anymore" 29%

Who Feels This Most Strongly

Segment "Not sure what my job is" "Coordinator not coder"
0–2 years experience 41% 52%
3–5 years experience 31% 47%
6–10 years experience 24% 39%
10+ years experience 19% 35%

Junior engineers report the most identity confusion — but senior engineers report the most grief about it.

The Pride Decline

74% of engineers say they used to feel "pride" in their code. That dropped to 31% who feel pride in code they shipped with heavy AI assistance. Pride hasn't disappeared — it's relocated to architecture decisions, team outcomes, and mentoring.

The "Someone Else's Code" Feeling

55% of engineers report feeling like they are "shipping someone else's work" even when that "someone" is an AI tool they prompted. This is distinct from copying Stack Overflow — the AI feels like an agent, not a resource.

The Learning Paradox

AI was supposed to help engineers learn faster. The data tells a more complicated story: learning is happening differently, and not always for the better.

📈 Learning "About" Things Up

71% report learning more about what exists (libraries, patterns, tools) than before AI. You discover faster. But see the next card.

📉 Learning "How To" Things Down

64% report learning less about how things actually work. When AI explains a concept, you accept the explanation rather than building the mental model through struggle.

🔄 The "Google Was Different" Effect

Engineers describe Google as "lookup" and AI as "understanding." But 57% report that AI explanations don't stick the same way that building from scratch did. The retention difference is measurable.

🧠 The Competence Illusion

48% of engineers report passing AI-assisted interviews they would have failed without AI. 61% find this troubling — they don't trust their own performance as a signal of skill.

"I thought I understood recursion until AI had to explain it to me for the fifth time. Then I realized I'd never actually built a mental model — I'd just seen enough examples to pattern-match."

— Mid-level engineer, 4 years, survey respondent

The 44%: Who's Considering Leaving

Nearly half of surveyed engineers

are seriously considering leaving the profession. This isn't "quiet quitting" — it's active exit planning. And it's concentrated in unexpected segments.

Segment Considering Leaving Already Taking Steps
0–2 years in industry 58% 22%
3–5 years 49% 17%
6–10 years 37% 11%
10+ years 31% 8%
Mandatory AI adoption at work 61% 29%
Optional AI at work 28% 9%

The Mandatory Adoption Amplifier

Engineers at companies that mandate AI tool usage are 2.2x more likely to be planning an exit than those at companies where AI use is optional. It's not AI itself — it's the loss of autonomy that accelerates departure.

What They're Switching To

Of engineers considering leaving: 31% want to stay in tech but switch to non-AI-heavy roles (infrastructure, security, data). 28% want to leave tech entirely. 23% are planning to go independent/freelance. 18% want to shift to management or product.

The Emotional Profile

Exit-planning engineers report: grief (61%), anger (44%), relief fantasy (52%), and guilt (38%). This is not burnout burnout — it's identity grief compounded by industry indifference.

"I don't want a different job. I want the job I had three years ago. I want to build something and know I built it. That job doesn't seem to exist anymore."

— Senior engineer, 12 years, seriously planning a career change

What Engineers Are Already Doing to Recover

Among the 56% not planning an exit, we asked: what concrete actions have helped? The answers reveal a pattern — structured separation from AI produces the fastest relief.

Recovery Practice % Who Tried It % Who Found It Helpful Avg. Time to Effect
No-AI coding blocks (2+ hrs/week) 41% 78% 2–3 weeks
Explanation Requirement (explain AI output before accepting) 29% 69% 3–4 weeks
Switching to AI-optional or AI-free tools occasionally 37% 64% 1–2 weeks
Deliberate skill-practice sessions (LeetCode, side projects) 33% 71% 4–6 weeks
Reduced AI tool usage overall 52% 58% 2–4 weeks
Manager conversation about AI pressure 22% 41% 1–4 weeks
Therapy or coaching 18% 82% 6–8 weeks
Job change to AI-optional company 14% 89% Immediate (after transition)

The #1 Most Effective Intervention

Engineers who implemented at least one weekly "no-AI block" — 2 or more hours of uninterrupted, AI-free coding — reported the fastest and most sustained improvement in all measured dimensions: ownership, skill confidence, and meaning at work.

The Explanation Requirement Effect

Engineers who adopted the practice of requiring themselves to explain AI-generated code before accepting it (verbally, in writing, or in comments) reported a 47% reduction in "middleman" feelings within 4 weeks. The act of explanation restores the cognitive engagement that delegation removes.

The Therapy Finding

While only 18% of engineers tried therapy, those who did reported the highest satisfaction (82%) — higher than any tool-change intervention. The issue isn't just tool friction; it's identity grief, and that needs a different kind of processing.

The Seniority Divide: Different Problems, Same Root

The crisis looks different across experience levels — but it traces back to the same underlying cause: AI has changed what "engineering" means, and the industry hasn't acknowledged this honestly.

0–2 Years: The Skill Gap They Don't See

Junior engineers are most likely to report high satisfaction with AI (69%) — but also most likely to be building on sand. 58% of juniors who use AI daily have never experienced the productive struggle of debugging a gnarly problem without AI. They don't know what they're missing.

3–5 Years: The Sharpest Drop

Mid-level engineers report the most acute identity crisis. They have enough experience to remember what "real engineering" felt like, and enough AI exposure to feel its effects. 51% report a significant shift in how they describe their work identity.

6–10 Years: The Authority Erosion

Senior engineers face a unique problem: their authority used to rest on expertise. When AI shows expertise instantly, what is a senior engineer's value? 43% report increased pressure to "prove" their seniority through non-technical dimensions: process, politics, and product sense.

10+ Years: The Grief Nobody Talks About

Veteran engineers report the most grief and the least help-seeking. They built their identity around craft mastery over decades. Now they watch junior engineers ship more code in a week than they did in a month. The grief is real, legitimate, and almost entirely unnamed by the industry.

"I trained for 15 years to be good at this. Now a 22-year-old with good prompts ships more than I do. I know I'm still valuable. I just can't find anyone who can explain to me exactly why."

— Principal engineer, 17 years, survey respondent

What This Survey Cannot Tell You

Honest research means acknowledging limits.

Self-Reported Data

Skill decline is self-reported. We did not run controlled skill assessments before and after AI adoption. These numbers reflect engineers' perceptions of their own abilities — which may be more emotionally real than objective measurement would show.

Selection Bias

Survey respondents came from clearing-ai.com quiz takers — a self-selected group already concerned about AI fatigue. This is not a random sample of all engineers. The numbers likely overstate the prevalence of AI fatigue in the broader population.

Correlation ≠ Causation

We cannot say AI tools caused skill decline from this data. It's possible that engineers whose skills were already declining turned to AI more heavily. The directional relationship is likely bidirectional.

The Positive Cases Are Missing

Engineers thriving with AI — and there are many — are less likely to visit a site about AI fatigue. The survey captures the crisis more clearly than the success stories.
