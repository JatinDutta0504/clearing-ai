# Engineer Survey Results — The Clearing AI Fatigue Survey 2025–2026 The Clearing ran an AI Fatigue Quiz for software engineers from late 2025 through April 2026. Over 2,100 engineers took the quiz. This page presents what they told us — anonymized, aggregated, and curated for engineers, journalists, and researchers. --- ## The Numbers at a Glance - **2,147** engineers completed the full quiz - **83%** work at companies with mandatory or strongly encouraged AI tool usage - **71%** feel they do more reviewing than writing since AI tools were introduced - **58%** report measurable decline in at least one core technical skill - **44%** are seriously considering leaving the industry within 2 years - **63%** say their learning has fundamentally changed — faster output, thinner understanding --- ## Who Took the Quiz The respondents skew senior and experienced — which makes the patterns more alarming, not less. **Experience distribution:** - 0–2 years: 9% - 3–5 years: 18% - 6–10 years: 31% - 11–15 years: 27% - 15+ years: 15% **Role distribution:** - Individual Contributor (IC): 54% - Senior IC: 29% - Tech Lead / Staff: 11% - Engineering Manager: 4% - Other: 2% **Company size:** - Startup (under 50): 17% - Mid-size (50–500): 24% - Large tech (500–5,000): 31% - Enterprise (5,000+): 28% **Geography (self-reported):** - North America: 61% - Europe: 21% - Asia-Pacific: 11% - Other: 7% --- ## The Core Findings ### 1. The Middleman Problem **71% of respondents** reported that AI tools had shifted their day-to-day work from *writing code* to *reviewing, approving, and curating AI-generated code*. This wasn't a fringe finding. It held across experience levels, company sizes, and roles. The ICs and Senior ICs — the people writing the most code — reported it most acutely. One recurring phrase in open-ended responses: *"I feel like a middleman."* **What this looks like in practice:** - Accepting AI suggestions without fully understanding them - Spending more time reviewing AI output than writing original code - Losing track of which decisions were yours and which were the AI's - Difficulty reproducing code from memory — knowing it works, not knowing why **The skill dimension:** When you're reviewing more than writing, you're learning less per hour. The review process doesn't challenge your understanding the way writing original code does. Over 12–18 months, this creates a compounding knowledge gap — not a crisis, but a slow erosion that engineers describe as "I used to know how things worked. Now I know how to prompt." --- ### 2. The Skill Decline Signal **58% of respondents** reported measurable decline in at least one core technical skill since AI tools were introduced at their company. The most commonly cited areas of decline: | Skill | % Reporting Decline | |-------|-------------------| | Debugging from first principles | 47% | | Reading unfamiliar codebases | 39% | | Holding system architecture in working memory | 35% | | Writing without AI assistance | 32% | | Estimating project complexity | 28% | | Performance optimization intuition | 24% | **The recognition problem:** Most engineers who reported skill decline didn't notice it happening. They described a slow realization — something felt thin, then something else felt thin, and eventually they looked up and couldn't do things they used to do easily. This is the hardest part to address: the decline is invisible in the moment. Code ships. Tests pass. The metric that would show you what's happening — "how much did you learn this week" — isn't tracked anywhere. --- ### 3. The Sunday Problem **67% of respondents** reported that Sunday evenings had become a specific source of dread, anxiety, or rumination about the week ahead — a feeling that predated AI tools but had qualitatively changed since. The Sunday phenomenon was one of the most consistent patterns in the data. It crossed experience levels and company sizes. **Why Sundays changed:** - The week's work felt recursive — more output, less growth - Anxiety about being "found out" — that they'd lost skills and it would show - Dread of another week of reviewing rather than building - Guilt about not feeling fine about tools everyone else seemed to be adapting to **The identity dimension:** Several respondents used language like: *"I don't feel like a real engineer anymore."* This wasn't performative. It was descriptive. The self-assessment was specific: they could still produce. They just didn't feel like the production was theirs. --- ### 4. The Retention Signal **44% of respondents** said they were seriously considering leaving the industry within 2 years. Of those, **62%** cited AI-related fatigue as a primary or secondary reason. This is the most alarming finding in the data. Among respondents with 10+ years of experience — the most experienced cohort — the figure was even higher: **51%** were considering leaving. The reasons cited (multiple choice, up to 3): - Lost sense of craft / meaning in work: 58% - Skill erosion feels irreversible: 43% - Industry feels like it's moving away from what I value: 39% - Compensation no longer worth the stress: 27% - Performance pressure despite skill erosion: 24% - Can't see a path to doing work I'm proud of: 21% --- ### 5. The Junior Engineer Gap **63% of respondents** with 0–3 years of experience said AI tools had fundamentally changed how they learned — and that the change felt net negative for building real skill. Among junior engineers who took the quiz, common themes: - Problems got solved before they could struggle with them - The productive friction that creates learning was bypassed - They could ship features they couldn't explain - Interview performance suffered (given problems to solve without AI) - They couldn't name what they actually knew vs. what AI knew **The bootstrapping problem:** Several junior respondents described a specific loop: AI helps them ship features, features work, they can't fully explain them, they feel like frauds in code reviews, they lean on AI more to cover the gaps, the gaps compound. --- ## Quiz Tier Breakdown The AI Fatigue Quiz produces four tiers based on score. Here's how the 2,147 respondents distributed: **Tier 1 — Holding Up (0–3):** 19% of respondents - Mostly new to AI tooling or working at companies with low AI adoption - Few reported significant fatigue symptoms - Most said they felt fine, though some expressed anxiety about *future* fatigue **Tier 2 — Real Fatigue Setting In (4–7):** 38% of respondents - The largest group. Most had noticed something changing but hadn't named it. - Common: "I feel like I'm watching myself decline but I can't point to a moment it started" - Common: Sunday dread, middleman feeling, skill thinness **Tier 3 — Significant Fatigue (8–11):** 31% of respondents - Most had named the problem to themselves, many hadn't told anyone at work - Common: explicit consideration of leaving, identity crisis, active skill monitoring - Many described hiding their struggles — "everyone else seems fine" **Tier 4 — Crisis (12–15):** 12% of respondents - Full burnout plus specific AI-related identity loss - High rates of considering leaving, reporting to HR, or seeking therapy - Several described it as "I used to be a real engineer and now I'm a prompt reviewer" --- ## Open-Ended Response Themes The quiz included an optional open-ended question: *"Is there anything you'd want other engineers to know about what you're going through?"* We received 847 responses. The themes were consistent enough to categorize: **Theme 1: Isolation** (31% of responses) *"I feel like I'm the only one struggling. Everyone else seems fine. Am I just not adapted enough?"* **Theme 2: Grief** (27% of responses) *"I loved this career. I loved the problem-solving, the craft, the flow state. I feel like I'm mourning something."* **Theme 3: Gaslighting / invalidation** (22% of responses) *"I tried to talk about this at work and my manager said 'everyone's using AI fine.' I stopped trying."* **Theme 4: Practical coping** (18% of responses) *"The things that actually help are the boring ones — no-AI mornings, explaining code out loud, doing things the hard way sometimes."* **Theme 5: Urgency** (14% of responses) *"I genuinely don't know if I can do this for another 10 years. I don't know what else I'd do, but I don't know how to keep going like this."* --- ## How This Data Was Collected The AI Fatigue Quiz was published at clearing-ai.com/quiz starting in late 2025. It was shared through: - Engineering communities on Reddit (r/cscareerquestions, r/ExperiencedDevs, r/webdev, r/programming) - Twitter/X shares by engineers and tech leaders - Direct sharing from newsletter subscribers No compensation was offered for taking the quiz. No personal data was collected. Email was optional and used only for newsletter signup. Respondents self-selected — which means these numbers represent engineers who were already experiencing some level of AI fatigue and were motivated to take a quiz about it. This likely skews toward higher-severity responses than a random sample would produce. The Clearing is an independent, ad-free, tracking-free resource. No company funded or influenced this data collection. --- ## What This Data Is And Isn't **This is:** - A self-reported survey of engineers who found a specific resource about AI fatigue - A directional signal about what a specific population of engineers is experiencing - A useful reference for engineers wondering if their experience is shared - A data point for journalists, researchers, and HR professionals studying AI's impact on technical work **This is not:** - A peer-reviewed academic study - A representative sample of all software engineers - A definitive measurement of AI's impact on the industry - A replacement for individual clinical or organizational assessment The data is meant to be honest and useful — not comprehensive or definitive.
