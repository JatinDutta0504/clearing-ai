## LinkedIn Post #1: "The Skill You Stopped Practicing"

---

Here's a question you can answer in 10 seconds:

What coding skill do you practice less now than you did two years ago?

Not "what's harder" — specifically: what did you used to do with your own hands that AI now does faster?

Most engineers have an answer within 3 seconds. They just don't like what it is.

---

The skill loss doesn't announce itself. You don't sit down one day and suddenly realize you can't debug the way you used to. It happens in the gaps — the moments where AI handles something and you move on without noticing what you just stopped learning.

The part that makes it worse: you usually can't measure the decline, because you weren't measuring the skill before. You just knew how to do things. Now you know how to prompt. These feel similar in the moment. They are not similar over 18 months.

---

The three most common losses I hear from engineers:

**Debugging.** When AI writes most of your code, you debug less. When you debug less, you read less of your own code in detail. When you read less, you understand less. This compounds until something breaks in a familiar area and you read your own code like someone else's for the first time.

**Architecture thinking.** It requires sitting with ambiguity long enough to see the shape of a solution. AI offers a shape immediately. Taking it means you stop sitting with the ambiguity. The muscle atrophies without use.

**Reading unfamiliar code.** Code written by others, in unfamiliar styles or older patterns, becomes harder to parse. Not because you're less capable — because you stopped practicing the translation work.

---

Here's the math no one runs:

If AI assists on 70% of your coding tasks, and you learn something from each task you do manually, you've gone from roughly 200 learning events per week to about 60. Over 2 years: roughly 14,500 fewer learning moments embedded in your work.

This isn't failure. It's just arithmetic.

---

The question isn't whether this happened. It's what you want to do about it.

The engineers navigating this well aren't the ones who rejected AI or embraced it uncritically. They're the ones who use it as a learning tool rather than an output delivery mechanism — and protect the time and space to still learn the things that matter.

If you want to see what 2,000+ engineers told us about this pattern — there's a quiz at clearing-ai.com that covers it. Takes 90 seconds.

## LinkedIn Post #2: "The Explanation Requirement"

---

Before accepting any AI suggestion in your code editor — before you accept it, before you review it, before you even read it carefully — complete this sentence out loud:

"I added this because..."

Not "the AI added this because." You.

Why is this the right approach? Why this pattern instead of another? What problem does it solve that you couldn't have solved yourself in the time it took to prompt?

If you can finish the sentence: the code is becoming yours.

If you can't — if the suggestion arrives, you understand that it works, and you couldn't have generated it yourself in the same time — you haven't learned the code. You've just received an answer.

---

Here's what I see happening:

When you accept an AI suggestion you don't understand, three things happen simultaneously:

1. You ship code you can't reproduce
2. You miss the learning opportunity that the problem would have provided
3. The gap between "working code" and "your understanding" grows — invisibly

This happens slowly enough that you don't notice until something breaks in that area three weeks later and you read your own code like someone else's. You know the feature works. You shipped it. And you have no idea why it works the way it does.

---

The 30-second rule:

You don't need to interrogate every line. You need to complete one honest sentence:

"I added this because..."

If you can't do it in 30 seconds — if the explanation isn't immediately available — that's data. It tells you this suggestion lives in a gap in your understanding.

Three options when you hit that gap:
- Investigate until you can explain it (slow, but educational)
- Reject the suggestion and solve it yourself (slowest, but most educational)
- Accept it with a note to return later (fastest, but risky — "later" rarely comes)

Option 3 is the trap most engineers fall into. The gap compounds.

---

The goal isn't to refuse AI suggestions. The goal is to use them as learning moments rather than output-delivery mechanisms.

The engineers who come to clearing-ai.com are mostly Tier 2-3 — they can see the gap growing and they want to tend to it before it becomes structural. The Explanation Requirement is one practice that addresses it directly.
