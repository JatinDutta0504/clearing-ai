# "The Explanation Requirement: The Practice That Separates Sharp Engineers from Stalled Ones"

---

I've been running an informal experiment with engineers who've been through our AI fatigue quiz.

We ask them one question:

*Can you explain what the last piece of AI-generated code does — without looking at it?*

The ones who can answer cleanly? They're the ones still growing.

The ones who hesitate, who have to go look? They're the ones who know something shifted — but can't name what.

---

Here's what I've noticed:

The engineers who maintain their edge have one non-negotiable practice:

**The Explanation Requirement.**

After every AI-assisted task, they close the tab. Then they explain what was just generated — out loud, in plain language, to no one in particular.

If they can't: that's the gap. That's the study plan.

If they can: they just got a rep in. That's learning.

---

Why this works is well-documented in learning science.

Retrieval practice — actively reconstructing what you know — is more effective than re-reading or reviewing. The AI gives you the output. The explanation requirement forces you to rebuild the path.

The gap between what AI generated and what you can explain is the actual curriculum.

---

The uncomfortable part:

Most engineers discover they can't explain things they thought they understood.

Not because they're not smart.
Because they stopped building the internal model and started trusting the external one.

This is how skill atrophy happens invisibly — the code ships, the tests pass, but the engineer's mental model quietly empties.

---

How to do it practically:

After any AI-assisted task:
1. Close the AI tab
2. Write one paragraph explaining what happened
3. Note where your mental model diverged from the AI's output

This takes 2 minutes.

Do it for 2 weeks. Notice when the gaps start getting smaller.

That's not coincidence. That's learning.

---

The engineers who navigate AI well have a simple rule:

They use AI to go faster on things they already understand.
They refuse to use AI to avoid understanding things they should.

The Explanation Requirement is how you tell the difference.
