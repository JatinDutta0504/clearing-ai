## Email Template 1: "Attention Residue — Why You Can't Focus After AI"

**Subject line:** Why your brain can't switch tasks after AI

**Preheader:** (23 min + 15 sec of cognitive cost. The math of interruption.)

---

**Email body:**

Hi [Name],

After you spend 30 minutes with an AI coding assistant, do you notice it takes *forever* to focus on something else?

You're not lazy. Your brain is just expensive.

**The Science:**

Gloria Mark at UC Irvine studied task switching in 2,300 person-hours of work observation. When engineers switch from one task to another, it takes an average of **23 minutes and 15 seconds** to mentally recover.

Not 2 minutes. Not 5 minutes. *23 minutes*.

But here's the part nobody talks about: AI makes that number *much worse*.

**Why?**

Traditional interruptions (Slack message, meeting) give your brain one switch to process.

AI interruptions give your brain *two*:

1. You stop mid-problem (the hardest moment of thinking — right when you had context)
2. You have to *evaluate* the output (which requires the same cognitive effort you just spent creating code)
3. You rapidly context-switch back

So it's not just one task switch. It's two task switches, back-to-back, with your attention split between "is this right?" and "where was I?"

**The Cost in Real Terms:**

If you context-switch 10 times per day (conservative for modern engineers):

- 10 switches × 23 min = **230 minutes of cognitive recovery debt**
- That's almost **4 hours of your 8-hour day** spent just recovering from task switches

And that's assuming you're only losing focus once per switch. Most engineers lose focus *multiple times* per switch.

**What Helps:**

1. **Batch your AI usage** into one 30-minute block instead of scattered through the day
2. **Complete micro-subtasks** before switching contexts (finish the function, not the line)
3. **Use transition rituals** (5-minute walk, water, reset) between AI work and deep work
4. **Protect 90-minute focus blocks** with zero interruptions
5. **Read every AI suggestion fully before committing** (don't switch until you understand)

This week's practice: Track how many times you context-switch to AI. Write down the time. See if your focus changes when you batch instead of scatter.

## Email Template 2: "The Explanation Requirement — How to Stay in Control"

**Subject line:** The one practice that changes everything

**Preheader:** (It's not 'use less AI'. It's this.)

---

**Email body:**

Hi [Name],

There's one practice that separates engineers who thrive with AI from those who burn out.

It's not complicated. It's not trendy. But it works.

**The Practice: The Explanation Requirement**

Before you commit AI-generated code to your codebase, you have to explain it. Out loud. In your own words. Like you're teaching it to someone.

Not "this looks right," but:

> "The algorithm does X because Y, which solves our problem Z. Here's why I chose this approach over A and B. This will break if [condition], so we need to watch for [signal]."

If you can't explain it, you don't understand it. And if you don't understand it, you're not really the engineer anymore — the AI is.

**Why This Matters:**

When you skip the explanation, something creeps in: *doubt.*

You ship code you didn't fully author. Your team reviews it. You explain it. But in the back of your mind: "Did *I* think of this, or did AI?"

Over time, that doubt becomes exhaustion. Then identity crisis. Then burnout.

The Explanation Requirement fixes this at the source. It forces you to *own* the code before it goes live.

**Three Levels:**

1. **Solo practice** (5 min): Explain to yourself out loud, or write it down. Clarity check.
2. **Peer review** (10 min): Explain to a teammate as part of code review. Natural teaching moment.
3. **Team meeting** (optional): Explain architectural decisions to the broader team. Authority signal.

**This Week:**

Pick one piece of AI-generated code you wrote recently. Explain it fully — out loud, like you're teaching it. Notice what gaps appear. Notice what feels solid.

That gap is where your learning lives.

## Email Template 3: "Skill Atrophy: When 'Using AI More' Makes You Worse"

**Subject line:** The trap that catches senior engineers

**Preheader:** (The more you use AI, the faster you lose skills. Here's why — and how to fix it.)

---

**Email body:**

Hi [Name],

There's a paradox in engineering right now:

The more effective you become with AI tools, the faster you lose the skills that made you valuable in the first place.

It's not laziness. It's automation.

**The Research:**

Lisanne Bainbridge (1983) studied "The Ironies of Automation":

> *The more a system is automated, the more the human operator has to be knowledgeable and alert to handle the unexpected.*

In other words: as you rely on AI more, you need to understand *more*, not less. But the opposite happens. You understand less. You trust the tool more. You practice less.

Over 6-12 months, critical skills atrophy:

- **Debugging instincts** — you learn to ask AI "what's wrong?" instead of seeing patterns
- **Algorithmic thinking** — you prompt instead of design
- **Error literacy** — you don't learn what mistakes mean anymore
- **Language internals** — you forget why things work, just that they do

**The danger:** When something breaks and AI can't help (unusual bug, new framework, architectural crisis), you're lost. And you *know* you're lost. That's when the identity crisis hits.

**What Senior Engineers Experience:**

If you've been coding 10+ years, this is *worse* for you, not better.

Your value was contextual judgment, pattern recognition, "knowing what to ask." If AI is doing the asking, your expertise becomes invisible even to you.

**How to Prevent It:**

1. **No-AI sessions** (2-4 hours/week): Code problems without AI. Let yourself struggle productively.
2. **Debug without AI first** (10 minutes): Try to find the bug before asking the tool.
3. **Read others' code** (1x/week): Learn how experienced engineers solve problems *without* AI.
4. **Teach** (formal or informal): Teaching forces you to know *why*, not just *how*.
5. **Quarterly skill audit** (once per month): Pick a skill you use daily. Test yourself without AI. How's your depth?

This Week:

Pick one skill that feels rusty (debugging? architecture? performance tuning?). Spend 30 minutes on it *without* AI. Notice where you're weak. That's your learning edge.

## Email Template 5: "Identity, Not Imposter Syndrome — The Difference Matters"

**Subject line:** This might not be what you think it is

**Preheader:** (The distinction that changes everything.)

---

**Email body:**

Hi [Name],

There's a conversation happening in your head:

> *"I'm not really an engineer anymore. I'm just someone who knows how to use AI. Anyone can do that."*

You might think that's imposter syndrome.

It's not.

**The Critical Difference:**

**Imposter syndrome** = cognitive distortion ("I'm not good enough, even though evidence says I am")

**Identity loss** = functional change ("I authored less code this quarter. I understand less about our system. My skills are diminishing.")

One is in your head. One is real.

And the recovery is completely different.

**How to Tell:**

Ask yourself: *"Do I have evidence that I'm less capable than I think I am?"*

If the answer is **no** (you're doing well at your job, people value your work, you get things done): **Imposter syndrome.** That's cognitive distortion. You might benefit from therapy or community.

If the answer is **yes** (you're shipping more, understanding less; you're faster but shallower; you can't debug what you don't understand): **Identity loss.** That's functional. You need to rebuild your skills and author ship, and that takes practice.

**Why This Matters:**

Because the fix is different:

- **Imposter syndrome fix:** Recognize your achievements, get community, push past self-doubt
- **Identity loss fix:** Rebuild skills (no-AI coding), return to authorship, reclaim your craft

If you treat identity loss like imposter syndrome, you'll keep doubting yourself forever. You need to actually rebuild.

**The Recovery:**

This is what Phase 2 of your recovery checklist is for. Extending boundaries. Building one skill back. Writing code end-to-end.

It's not comfortable. But it's real.
