# The Dispatch #7 — "The Debugger's Drift"
**Send date:** Monday, April 14, 2026, 9:00 AM PDT
**Subject line:** The one skill AI is quietly destroying (and how to get it back)

---

Hi [[NAME]],

Two weeks ago, a senior engineer told me something that stuck with me.

*"I can build anything now. I ship more code than I ever have. But when something breaks at 2am — really breaks — I have no idea what's happening. I just prompt-storm until something sticks."*

He has 12 years of experience. He is not alone.

This is what we're calling **The Debugger's Drift** — the slow erosion of the skill that separates engineers from code assemblers.

---

## The paradox nobody talks about

AI makes you faster at *writing* code. It makes you slower at *understanding* it.

Here's why: every time AI handles a tricky bug, you learn that a tricky bug has a solution. But you don't learn *how the solution was found*. You learn the answer. You lose the diagnosis.

And here's what nobody tells you — AI-generated code is *optimistically written*. It assumes the happy path. It doesn't know about the edge case your team discovered in production at 4pm on a Friday three months ago. So when something breaks, the AI's explanation is often plausible but incomplete.

And you can't tell the difference.

---

## Why this hurts seniors most

There's a counterintuitive finding from cognitive science research: the more expertise you have, the *more* you rely on context — years of patterns, failure modes, edge cases you've seen. AI doesn't just speed up your work. It strips away the contextual scaffolding that your expertise rests on.

The result: senior engineers often feel the drift more acutely than juniors. You know something's off. You can't quite name it.

---

## The Explanation Requirement

Here's the one practice that reverses this:

**Before you accept any AI suggestion — explain it out loud. In your own words. As if you're teaching it to a colleague.**

"I fixed the null pointer by adding a guard clause because..."

"I refactored this loop because when the list is empty it would..."

If you can't explain it in a complete sentence — you're not done debugging. The AI bought you a plausible answer, not understanding.

This is uncomfortable. It will slow you down. It's also the only thing that keeps the skill from atrophying.

---

## One concrete test

Next time something breaks and AI gives you a fix: before you apply it, ask yourself:

*"Do I know why this fixes it?"*

If the answer is "it just does" — you're debugging with a blindfold on.
