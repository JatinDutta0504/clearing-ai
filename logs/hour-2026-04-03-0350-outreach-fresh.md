## HN ENGAGEMENT PREP (for when you submit)
Top-level comment angles to have ready:

**Angle 1 (identity/ownership):**
The 63% "middleman" stat is the one that hit me hardest when we analyzed the data. It's not imposter syndrome — it's a functional change in what it means to author code. Your relationship to your work changed, whether you signed off on it or not.

**Angle 2 (juniors most at risk):**
The people most at risk aren't seniors who learned without AI. It's 3-5 year engineers who started learning with AI and never got the "struggle phase" that builds the intuition they're now missing. The foundation was built on AI-assisted ground.

**Angle 3 (data honesty):**
Worth noting: 2,047 is our quiz data, not a formal peer-reviewed study. We're transparent about that on the site. But the pattern matches what occupational psychology has documented in automation transitions for 40 years — this isn't new, just newly applicable to software.

**Angle 4 (structural vs personal):**
The framing that "you just need better habits" misses the point. This is a structural problem. The incentives that push AI adoption (velocity, metrics, competitive pressure) are the same ones that create the fatigue. Individual recovery tactics help. Organizational change is what actually solves it.

**Angle 5 (response to "just take breaks"):**
Breaves don't fix skill atrophy. Rest helps burnout. But if the muscle isn't being exercised because AI does the hard parts, more vacation just means you're well-rested while the gap keeps growing.
