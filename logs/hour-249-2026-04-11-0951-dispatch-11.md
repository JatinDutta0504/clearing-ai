# The Dispatch #11 — "The Middleman Problem"
**Send date:** Monday, April 21, 2026, 9:00 AM PDT
**Theme:** Why shipping more code with AI can leave you feeling like a middleman — and the specific practices that restore actual authorship

---

Hi [[NAME]],

Here's something that's showing up constantly in the conversations we have with engineers who take the AI Fatigue Quiz:

You're shipping more than ever. But you feel like less.

Not imposter syndrome. Not burnout. Something more specific: **the middleman problem.**

---

## What the middleman problem actually is

In traditional software engineering, there was a direct line between you and the work.

You understood the problem. You designed the solution. You wrote the code. You shipped it. You could trace every decision back to a conversation you had, a trade-off you made, a mistake you learned from.

That line is broken now.

You understand the problem. AI generates the solution. You review it. You ship it. The outcome is your responsibility — but the thinking isn't fully yours. You're vouching for work you didn't fully generate.

This creates a particular kind of exhaustion. Not from hard work. From **responsibility without understanding.**

It compounds. After months of this, you start to feel like a quality assurance checkpoint for someone else's thinking. Not a craftsperson. Not a builder. A curator.

---

## Why "just be more intentional" doesn't fix it

The obvious advice is: *review the AI output more carefully, understand what it's doing, make sure you're learning from it.*

This is good advice. It's also incomplete.

The problem isn't that you're not paying attention. It's that the feedback loop that used to create understanding — the struggle, the debugging, the "why did that break?" — has been moved upstream into the AI's process, not yours.

You can't retrieve what you didn't generate.

The understanding doesn't come from reviewing the output. It comes from the work of getting there. And when AI does that work for you, the understanding doesn't transfer automatically. It just... doesn't happen.

---

## The Author Test

Here's a question worth sitting with this week:

*When you look at a feature you shipped last week — something significant, not a quick fix — how much of it could you explain from memory? Not the structure. The reasoning. Why that approach and not another one. What the trade-offs were. What you learned.*

If the answer is "less than I'd like," that's not a character flaw. That's a signal.

The gap between shipping and understanding is the middleman problem. And it doesn't close on its own.

---

## One practice that helps

There's a technique that consistently shows up in recovery stories from engineers who have worked through this:

**The Explanation Requirement.**

Before you ship any AI-generated code — even after reviewing it — write a one-sentence explanation of *why* this code does what it does, in your own words, without reference to the prompt or the output.

Not what the code does. Why that approach.

This seems small. It's not. It forces a translation step between "AI output reviewed" and "your understanding." It doesn't fully recreate the learning loop — nothing does except doing the work — but it creates a friction that keeps the gap visible.

If you can't explain it in one sentence, that's information. Dig deeper or rebuild that piece without AI.

---

## What we know from the data

In our AI Fatigue Quiz, taken by over 2,000 engineers:

- **63%** said they sometimes feel like a "middleman" between AI output and actual work
- **71%** reported they took the quiz because something concrete happened — a moment where they realized they didn't know something they felt they should
- **58%** noticed measurable skill changes — debugging ability, reading unfamiliar code, working without AI help

These aren't weak signals. They're a pattern.

The middleman problem is real, widespread, and not going away on its own.

---

## One thing you can do this week

Before your next code review, pick one significant piece of AI-generated code and spend five minutes explaining it to yourself — not what it does, but why the code is shaped the way it is.

Notice how much of the *why* you can access.

If that number feels lower than you'd like, that's the gap. The gap is real. And closing it is a practice, not a realization.
