# The Dispatch #14 — "The Middleman Problem"
**Theme:** 71% of engineers feel like middlemen — reviewing code they didn't write. Here's why that's not imposter syndrome.

---

Hi [[NAME]],

Last week, we askedengineers a simple question:

*When you ship code that an AI wrote — how much of it could you reproduce from scratch right now?*

The answers were... illuminating.

71% said: less than half.
44% said: almost none.

These aren't junior engineers. These are experienced developers who are paying close attention to what AI tooling has quietly done to their relationship with their own work.

**This is the middleman problem.**

---

## What the middleman problem actually is

A middleman evaluates output. They don't create it. They approve it or reject it based on criteria they understand.

When you spend 18 months as an AI-assisted engineer, something quiet happens: you get very good at evaluating AI-generated code. You develop a quick sense of whether the suggestion is plausible, whether it fits the codebase, whether it solves the problem.

What you might lose — slowly enough not to notice until it's significant — is the ability to generate the original.

Not because you got dumber. Because the generative work atrophied from disuse.

This is different from imposter syndrome. Imposter syndrome is about perception distorted by anxiety: you can do the work, but you feel like a fraud.

The middleman problem is functional. You genuinely can't do what you could do 18 months ago — not without significant recalibration.

---

## Why it's hard to address

The middleman problem is invisible from the outside.

Your velocity looks fine. Your output looks fine. Your performance review is fine. The gap between "you with AI" and "you without AI" grows so slowly that you don't notice until someone asks: *Could you build this without AI tools?*

And you realize you don't know.

The first signal is usually some version of this:

You ship a feature. It works. The code looks reasonable. And three weeks later when something breaks in that area, you have no idea why. You're reading your own code like someone else's. You're debugging output you didn't generate.

The question starts forming: *Am I still actually a developer?*

---

## Why it's not imposter syndrome

Imposter syndrome says: *I can do this but I feel like a fraud.*

The middleman problem says: *Something has actually changed about what I can do.*

One is a perception problem. The other is a functional change.

The distinction matters because the solutions are different.

Imposter syndrome: reassurance, evidence, reframing.
Middleman problem: deliberate practice, calibration, no-AI sessions.

Mixing them up leads to well-meaning advice that doesn't help: *"You just need to trust yourself more!"* When the actual problem is structural, not psychological.

We wrote a longer breakdown of the difference at clearing-ai.com/imposter-syndrome-vs-ai-fatigue — because getting the diagnosis right is the first step toward actually fixing it.

---

## One practice that actually helps

The Explanation Requirement.

Before accepting any AI suggestion, complete this sentence out loud:

*"I added this because..."*

Not "the AI added this because." You. Why is this the right approach? What problem does it solve? What would break if you removed it?

If you can finish the sentence, the code is becoming yours.

If you can't — if the suggestion arrives and you understand that it works but not why — you haven't learned the code. You've just received an answer.

This practice is slower. It's uncomfortable. It interrupts the flow.

It's also the only thing I've found that reliably keeps the generative muscles from atrophying while still using AI tools.

## What we know from 2,000+ quiz takers

The engineers who come to clearing-ai.com tend to cluster into four tiers:

**Tier 1 (Mild):** You notice AI helps a lot. You're occasionally aware that you're learning differently. Generally fine.

**Tier 2 (Real fatigue is setting in):** Sunday dread. Skill uncertainty. A growing gap between what you can explain and what you've shipped.

**Tier 3 (Significant):** Code ownership has changed. You can't reproduce your own work. The explanation test is getting harder to pass.

**Tier 4 (Critical):** You're considering leaving the industry. This isn't just a tool problem — it's an identity problem. The craft you built your professional self around feels gone.

The majority of quiz takers land in Tier 2-3. They're not struggling because they're lazy or resistant to change. They're struggling because they've noticed something real — and they don't have language for it yet.

## One honest question

Next time you accept an AI suggestion: pause for 30 seconds.

Can you explain why it's right?

Not just that it works — why this approach, why this pattern, why this edge case handling.

The answer is data. It's information about where you are. The gap between "AI said it" and "I know why" is worth watching.

Not to panic about. To tend to.
