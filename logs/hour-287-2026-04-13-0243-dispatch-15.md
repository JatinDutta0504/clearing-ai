# The Dispatch #15 — "The Explanation Requirement"
**Theme:** Before accepting any AI suggestion — complete this sentence: "I added this because..." If you can't, you haven't learned the code. You've just received an answer.

---

Hi [[NAME]],

There's a test you can run on your own AI usage right now.

Next time you accept an AI suggestion in your code editor — before you accept it, before you review it, before you even read it carefully — stop and complete this sentence out loud:

*"I added this because..."*

Not "the AI added this because." You. Why is this the right approach? Why this pattern instead of another? What problem does it solve that you couldn't have solved yourself in the time it took to prompt?

If you can finish the sentence: the code is becoming yours.

If you can't — if the suggestion arrives, you understand that it works, and you couldn't have generated it yourself in the same time — you haven't learned the code. You've just received an answer.

**This is the Explanation Requirement.**

---

## Why it matters more than you think

The goal isn't to refuse AI suggestions. The goal is to use them as learning moments rather than output-delivery mechanisms.

Here's the problem: when you accept an AI suggestion you don't understand, three things happen simultaneously:

1. You ship code you can't reproduce
2. You miss the learning opportunity that the problem would have provided
3. The gap between "working code" and "your understanding" grows — invisibly

This happens slowly enough that you don't notice until something breaks in that area three weeks later and you read your own code like someone else's. You know the feature works. You shipped it. And you have no idea why it works the way it does.

The Explanation Requirement is a friction device. It slows you down. It makes accepting AI suggestions less fluid.

That's the point.

---

## What it actually looks like in practice

The Explanation Requirement sounds like this in your head:

*"I added this try-catch block because... hmm. Why is this the right place for it? What exception is it handling that would have crashed the system? Oh — it's handling the case where the API call fails mid-stream. And if that happens mid-stream, what state is the request object in? Is it still valid for retry?"*

If you can go that deep: the code is becoming part of your mental model.

If you can't — if the explanation is something like "AI added it, probably for error handling, seems reasonable" — you've accepted output without understanding. That's fine once. It's a problem when it's every time.

---

## The 30-second rule

You don't need to interrogate every line. You need to complete one honest sentence.

*"I added this because..."*

If you can't do it in 30 seconds — if the explanation isn't immediately available — that's data. It tells you this suggestion lives in a gap in your understanding.

You have three options:
1. Investigate until you can explain it (slow, but educational)
2. Reject the suggestion and solve it yourself (slowest, but most educational)
3. Accept it with a note to return later (fastest, but risky — "later" rarely comes)

Option 3 is the trap most engineers fall into. The gap compounds.

---

## The weekly calibration

Once a week — Friday afternoon works well — review your week's PRs. For each significant change, ask: *Could I explain why this approach was taken without referencing the code?*

If yes: the work is becoming yours.
If no: you've had another week of output without learning.

This isn't about guilt. It's about noticing the compounding. The engineers who come to clearing-ai.com are mostly Tier 2-3 — they can see the gap growing and they want to tend to it before it becomes structural.
