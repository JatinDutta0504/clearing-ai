# The Dispatch #16 — "The Skill You Stopped Practicing"
**Theme:** AI has quietly replaced a skill you used to have. You didn't notice until you tried to do it without AI — and couldn't.

---

Hi [[NAME]],

Here's a question you can answer in 10 seconds:

What coding skill do you practice less now than you did two years ago?

Not "what's harder" or "what's worse" — specifically: what did you used to do with your own hands that AI now does faster?

Most engineers have an answer within 3 seconds. They just don't like what it is.

---

## The Compounding Invisible

The skill loss doesn't announce itself. There's no moment where you sit down to write a function and suddenly realize you can't. It happens in the gaps — the moments where AI handles something and you move on without noticing what you just stopped learning.

This is the compounding invisible. You don't see the learning you're not getting. You only eventually notice the understanding that didn't accumulate.

Here's the part that makes it worse: you usually can't measure the decline, because you weren't measuring the skill before. You just knew how to do things. Now you know how to prompt. These feel similar in the moment. They are not similar over 18 months.

---

## The Three Most Common Losses

**Debugging.** The most cited loss. When AI writes most of your code, you debug less. When you debug less, you read less of your own code in detail. When you read less, you understand less. This compounds until something breaks in a familiar area and you read your own code like someone else's for the first time.

**Architecture thinking.** It requires sitting with ambiguity long enough to see the shape of a solution. AI offers a shape immediately. Taking it means you stop sitting with the ambiguity. Taking it enough times means the ambiguity stays — not because you chose it, but because the muscle atrophied without use.

**Reading陌生 code.** Code written by others, in unfamiliar styles or older patterns, becomes harder to parse. Not because you're less capable — because you stopped practicing the translation work. You were good at it once. You haven't done it in a while.

---

## What Two Years Actually Looks Like

Here's the math no one runs:

If AI assists on 70% of your coding tasks, and you learn something from each task you do manually, you've gone from ~200 learning events per week to ~60. Over 2 years: roughly 14,500 fewer learning moments embedded in your work.

This isn't failure. It's just arithmetic.

The question isn't whether this happened. It's what you want to do about it.

---

## The Practice Gap Triage

You don't need to reject AI. You need to notice which gap is costing you the most — in confidence, in work quality, in the feeling that you're still the engineer you were.

Three questions:

1. **What did I used to be able to do in 20 minutes that now takes me 45 minutes without AI?** That's your skill gap. Start there.

2. **What do I avoid looking at carefully because AI generated most of it?** That's your confidence gap. Start there.

3. **What decision would I make differently if I understood this system better?** That's your understanding gap. That's the expensive one.

---

## One Practice That Addresses All Three

The Explanation Requirement — from last week's Dispatch.

Before accepting any AI suggestion: complete the sentence "I added this because..." out loud.

The goal isn't to refuse suggestions. It's to notice which suggestions close a gap in your understanding and which ones just deliver output. One builds your model of the system. One doesn't.

---

## What 2,000+ Quiz Takers Told Us

The most common Tier 2 experience — the engineers in the middle of AI fatigue — is some version of this:

*"I can see that I'm shipping more than I used to. And I can feel that I'm learning less."*

This is the compounding invisible showing up in self-awareness. They know something is off. They just don't have a name for it yet.

Having the name helps. The next step is the practice.
