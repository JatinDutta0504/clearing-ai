# The Dispatch #17 — "The Middleman Problem"

**Theme:** You shipped the code. Your name is on it. But somewhere between the prompt and the merge, the authorship got... thin.

---

Hi [[NAME]],

Here's something that shows up in our quiz data at every seniority level:

*"I write more code than I used to. And I understand less of it."*

Not always. Not every day. But enough that it sits in the background — the quiet suspicion that you're shepherding AI output more than building things yourself.

There's a specific name for this. Engineers who've found it call it different things. But the most accurate phrase we've heard:

**The middleman problem.**

You're in the middle of a process you used to own. The AI generates. You evaluate. You ship. The code works. The PR gets approved. Everything functions.

But the felt sense of authorship — *I built this, I understand it, I could explain every part of why it works* — that's the thing that quietly went missing.

---

## What "Middleman" Actually Means

The middleman isn't a failure mode. It's a structural shift.

When a human code reviewer gives feedback, there's a conversation. You defend choices, push back, refine. The back-and-forth tightens your understanding with each exchange. By the time code ships, you've been through the reasoning multiple times.

When AI generates, the reasoning is hidden inside the output. You evaluate plausibility — does this look right? Does it match what I expected? — rather than building understanding through dialogue.

The gap between "accepted" and "understood" is where the middleman problem lives.

Over months and years, that gap can get very wide without you noticing. The code works. The tests pass. The velocity metrics look great. And underneath, the authorship feeling quietly fades.

---

## The Experience Doesn't Match the Performance

Here's what's strange about this particular fatigue:

On every external measure, you're doing fine. You're productive. You're shipping. You're getting code reviews approved. By the numbers, nothing is broken.

But inside — there's a subtler signal. You open a file you wrote three months ago and it reads like someone else's code. You can't explain, from scratch, why a particular approach was chosen. You know the AI suggested it and you agreed.

This isn't imposter syndrome. Imposter syndrome is "they'll find out I'm not as good as they think." The middleman problem is different: "I shipped something real and I'm not sure how much of it is mine."

Both can be true at once. But they need different responses.

---

## The One Question That Closes the Gap

Before you accept any AI suggestion: complete the sentence.

*"I added this because..."*

Not what it does. Why it's the right approach given everything else in the system. What alternatives you considered. What would break if you changed it.

If you can complete the sentence fluently: the authorship loop is intact. The AI helped you see something you already understood.

If you can't: the AI just delivered output. The gap just widened by one decision.

This is why the Explanation Requirement is the single most useful recovery practice we've found. It doesn't restrict AI use. It creates a forcing function that reveals exactly where the authorship is and isn't.

Over time, the engineers who practice it report something specific: they start noticing which AI outputs teach them something and which ones just... solve the problem. The distinction becomes visible in real time.

---

## What 2,000+ Quiz Takers Showed Us

The Tier 2 / Tier 3 engineers — the ones in the middle of AI fatigue — describe some version of this:

- They can see their output increasing while their confidence in their own abilities quietly declines
- They've started to avoid looking too closely at AI-generated code — because if they look, they realize how much they don't understand
- They've noticed they can no longer estimate how long something will take without AI — because they've stopped calibrating against their own intuition
- They feel a subtle guilt about all of the above that they can't quite name

The middleman problem is a feature of the current state of AI tooling, not a character flaw. And it has a specific remedy: closing the authorship loop on each decision, one sentence at a time.

---

## One Week Trial

This week: every time you accept an AI suggestion, pause and complete the sentence out loud.

Not as a ritual. As a calibration.

If you can do it easily — your authorship loop is working. If you can't — that's your signal. That's the gap. That's where the attention goes next.

No productivity tools, no tracking, no journal required. Just one question.
