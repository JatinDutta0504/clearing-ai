# The Dispatch #18 — "The Calibration Drift"

**Theme:** Your internal sense of what's hard versus what's easy has quietly drifted. You don't notice until you try to estimate without AI — and realize your calibration is gone.

---

Hi [[NAME]],

Try something for me.

Pick a coding task you did yesterday or today. Not a big one — something small. A function, a refactor, a bug you chased down.

Now try to answer this without AI:

*If I had to build this from scratch — no Copilot, no Claude, no ChatGPT — how long would this actually take me?*

Not a rough guess. An actual estimate based on your felt sense of the problem's complexity.

Most engineers who try this report the same thing: they can't do it. Not "I'm not sure" — genuinely cannot access the estimation. The sense of how hard something is has become inseparable from how hard it is with AI assistance. The two have fused.

This is the calibration drift.

---

## What Calibration Actually Means

Calibration is your internal model of difficulty.

You built it over thousands of hours: debugging in an IDE without AI suggestions, writing functions from memory, estimating complexity from reading code. You learned, roughly, how long things take because you'd done them enough times to feel the weight of them.

This model is useful. It lets you plan. It tells you when something is wrong — when an approach feels easier than it should, or harder. It's the difference between intuition and guesswork.

When AI starts handling the parts you used to do manually, the calibration starts to drift. Not because you're getting worse — because the reference points are gone.

You no longer have thousands of hours of data about how hard things actually are without AI help.

---

## The Estimation Problem Compound

Here's why the drift matters beyond the philosophical:

When you can't estimate without AI, you can't do rough order-of-magnitude reasoning. You can't know if a task is 2 hours or 2 days until AI generates it. You can't look at a pull request and immediately sense whether the approach is right — you have to run the AI output and see what happens.

Engineers describe this as "flying blind." The map and the terrain used to match. Now the AI generates the map and you navigate the terrain, and the two don't always line up.

This shows up in job interviews. Senior engineers who sailed through system design 3 years ago now find themselves reaching for AI to reason through architecture problems they'd once done cold. Not because they're less capable — because the muscle memory of working through hard problems without assistance has weakened.

This is the calibration drift. It's real. And it's not your fault.

---

## The Drift Is Not Linear

One of the strangest features of this drift: it's not uniform.

You might have excellent calibration in some areas — where you've spent a decade building intuition — and no calibration at all in others. A senior engineer might still estimate debugging timelines well (because debugging still requires human judgment) but be completely unable to estimate implementation time without AI (because implementation is mostly AI-generated now).

This creates an odd asymmetry: your confidence in your expertise is paradoxically high in areas where AI helps most — because AI outputs look polished and correct — and paradoxically low in areas where you'd actually be most capable.

You know more than you think. But you can't feel it in the ways you used to.

---

## The One Test That Reveals the Drift

Pick a problem you've been meaning to think through. Could be architectural, could be a design decision, could be a tricky bug.

Before you open any AI tool: write down, in plain English, what you think is going on. Not what you want AI to tell you. What your actual hypothesis is.

Then use AI. Then compare.

Does your hypothesis appear in the AI's response? Was it in the right direction even if incomplete? Did the AI suggest something you'd have ruled out based on experience?

If your hypothesis was in the ballpark: your calibration is intact in that area. The AI is accelerating you, not replacing your judgment.

If your hypothesis was completely off: that's the drift. That's the gap. And that's the signal for where to slow down.

This isn't about rejecting AI. It's about knowing which parts of your judgment are still calibrated and which parts are running on AI output as a proxy.

---

## What 2,000+ Quiz Takers Showed Us

The engineers who score highest on AI fatigue — Tier 3 and Tier 4 — almost uniformly report this:

*"I used to be able to look at a problem and have a pretty good sense of whether my approach was right. Now I just generate something and see if it works."*

*"I can't tell anymore if I'm good at this or if the tools are good."*

*"I used to have opinions about code. Now I mostly have opinions about whether the AI suggestion seems reasonable."*

These aren't failures of intelligence or care. They're the natural result of a shift in where the work happens. The reasoning used to happen in your head. Now it happens in the AI's output. And the output looks good — so the gap stays invisible.

The recovery isn't using less AI. It's being deliberate about which reasoning you do yourself and which you delegate. The calibration comes back, slowly, when you practice the distinction.

---

## One Practice This Week

Before you use AI on your next task: write three things you expect the answer to look like.

Not what the code will do — what approach you'd take, what the tradeoffs would be, what you'd rule out.

Then use AI. Then notice: was your mental model in the right space? Did the AI's approach surprise you? Were there things you knew that the AI didn't consider?

Over time, this rebuilds the calibration. Not to 100% — maybe not even to where it was. But enough that you can feel the difference between navigating and just watching the navigation happen.
