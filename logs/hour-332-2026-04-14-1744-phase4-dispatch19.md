## Dispatch #19 — "The Estimation Gap"

Try something before you open any AI tool.

Pick a task you did recently. A function, a refactor, a tricky line. Now try to answer this:

*If I had to build this from scratch — no Copilot, no Claude — how long would this take?*

Not a rough guess. An actual estimate based on your felt sense of the problem.

Most engineers who try this can't do it. Not "I'm not sure" — genuinely cannot access the estimation. The sense of how hard things are has fused with AI assistance. You no longer have thousands of hours of data about how hard things are without the tool.

This is the estimation gap.

---

### Why It Matters Beyond Planning

When you can't estimate without AI, you lose the map-and-terrain match. You used to navigate and the AI was one tool among many. Now the AI generates the map and you navigate the terrain, and the two don't always line up.

This shows up in interviews. Senior engineers who sailed through system design 3 years ago now reach for AI to reason through architecture problems they'd once done cold. Not because they're less capable — because the working-through-hard-problems muscle has weakened from disuse.

It's also in code reviews. You used to look at a PR and immediately sense whether the approach was right. Now you run the AI output and see what happens. The tests pass. But whether the approach is sound — that's less legible than it used to be.

---

### The Test

Before you use AI on your next task: write down what you expect the answer to look like. Not what the code will do — what approach you'd take, what you'd rule out.

Then use AI. Then compare.

Was your hypothesis in the ballpark? Did the AI suggest something you'd have ruled out?

If yes: your calibration is intact in that area. The AI is accelerating you, not replacing your judgment.

If no: that's the gap. That's the signal for where to slow down and practice.

---

### What 2,000+ Quiz Takers Showed

The engineers scoring highest on AI fatigue almost uniformly report:

*"I used to be able to look at a problem and have a pretty good sense of whether my approach was right. Now I just generate something and see if it works."*

*"I can't tell anymore if I'm good at this or if the tools are good."*

*"I used to have opinions about code. Now I mostly have opinions about whether the AI suggestion seems reasonable."*

These aren't failures of intelligence. They're the natural result of where the reasoning now happens — in the AI's output, not your head.

---

### One Practice This Week

Before you use AI on your next task: write three things you expect the answer to look like. Then use AI. Then notice: was your mental model in the right space? Did the AI's approach surprise you?

Over time, this rebuilds the calibration. Not to 100% — maybe not to where it was. But enough that you can feel the difference between navigating and watching the navigation happen.
