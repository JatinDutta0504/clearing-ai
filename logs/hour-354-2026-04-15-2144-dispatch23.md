# Dispatch #23 — "The Estimation Problem"

**Subject:** Why your estimates got way too optimistic (and it's not a character flaw)
**Send date:** Thursday, April 30, 2026
**Issue #:** 23

---

<p>Three years ago, you estimated features in days and you were usually right within a factor of two.</p>

<p>Six months ago, you started using AI coding tools seriously. Your velocity went up. Your estimates went down. You thought: finally, I'm getting faster.</p>

<p>Then the sprint retrospectives started showing the pattern.</p>

<p>The tickets closed in half the estimated time. The code was shipped. The code reviews looked fine. And when something broke three weeks later, you had no idea why.</p>

<p><strong>Your estimates did not get better. Your calibration got worse.</strong></p>

<h3>What actually happened to your estimation</h3>

<p>Traditional estimation has a built-in accuracy mechanism: you know roughly how long things take because you have done them. You have debugged code you wrote. You have estimated features with your own hands. You have a felt sense of your unknown unknowns.</p>

<p>AI tooling quietly removes that mechanism.</p>

<p>When AI handles the implementation, debugging becomes faster. Edge cases are pre-handled by optimistic code. Unknown unknowns are hidden behind plausible output that looks correct in the review. You ship faster. The estimation looks better.</p>

<p>But the accuracy was never in the estimate. It was in the calibration — the relationship between what you knew and what you were estimating. That relationship has been severed.</p>

<h3>The specific failure mode nobody names</h3>

<p>There are two ways estimation degrades:</p>

<p><strong>Burnout.</strong> You are exhausted, you are slower, your estimates go up. This is visible. This is fixable with rest.</p>

<p><strong>AI-assisted estimation corruption.</strong> You are faster, your estimates go down, and you have lost the calibration that made them accurate. This is invisible from the outside. Your velocity metrics look great. Your retrospectives show short cycle times. Nobody flags it as a problem until something breaks in production and the on-call engineer realizes they cannot trace the failure back to anything in their mental model.</p>

<p>You are not worse at estimating. You have been cut off from the feedback loop that made your estimates accurate.</p>

<h3>The test you can run right now</h3>

<p>Go to your last 10 closed tickets. For each one, ask yourself:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Could I have estimated this without AI?</strong> Not: should I have. Could I have.</li>
<li><strong>What would my estimate have been without AI?</strong> Was it shorter, longer, or the same as the actual?</li>
<li><strong>If I had to estimate it again today without AI, what would I say?</strong></li>
</ul>

<p>Most engineers who run this test find something specific: their AI-assisted estimates are 30-50% shorter than their unaided estimates would be. Not because they got faster. Because they lost the parts of the problem that used to make estimation hard.</p>

<h3>One practice that helps</h3>

<p><strong>Weekly unaided estimation sessions.</strong></p>

<p>Once a week, before you open any AI tools, take one ticket from the backlog and estimate it by hand. Write down the number. Then open it up, use AI, build it, ship it. Compare your estimate to what actually happened.</p>

<p>Track the ratio: unaided estimate vs actual. Week over week, you will see whether your calibration is improving or degrading.</p>

<p>This is not about accuracy for its own sake. It is about staying connected to the relationship between your judgment and the work. That relationship is what makes you an engineer and not a code curator.</p>

<h3>What 2,000+ engineers told us</h3>

<p>71% of quiz takers said they could not explain most of the code they shipped last week.</p>

<p>44% said they are considering leaving tech entirely — not because of burnout, but because they have noticed the gap between what they ship and what they understand.</p>

<p>The estimation problem is not a character flaw. It is a structural feature of how AI tooling interacts with the feedback loop that makes engineering judgment accurate.</p>
