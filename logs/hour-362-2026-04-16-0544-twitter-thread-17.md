## Thread Structure

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
```
Gloria Mark's research team watched engineers work for 20 years.
What they found is one of the most depressing statistics I've ever read:

After every interruption — even one lasting just a minute — it takes 
an average of 23 minutes and 15 seconds to fully regain focus.

Not 2 minutes. Not 5 minutes.

23 minutes.

That's not a productivity tip. That's a structural indictment of how 
modern software teams operate.

🧵 A thread on what this means for AI-assisted work 👇
```

**Tweet 2 (The AI Complication):**
```
Here's what makes AI assistance both valuable and dangerous:

AI reduces some interruptions (no more "let me Google that"). 
But it introduces new ones — constant suggestion popups, 
multi-option outputs to evaluate, context-switching between 
your mental model and the AI's suggestions.

You're not interrupted by *asking* AI.
You're interrupted by *evaluating* AI's answers.

That evaluation is itself a cognitively costly interruption.
```

**Tweet 3 (The Compounding):**
```
Do the math:

If you interact with AI tools 15 times per workday:
— Each interaction: 2-5 min of evaluation + integration
— Each causes a 23-min focus recovery
— You're effectively in a state of partial focus recovery 
   for most of your workday

This is what "Sunday dread" and "I worked all day but 
didn't feel like I got anything done" feel like structurally.

It's not you. It's the interruption architecture.
```

**Tweet 4 (The Invisible Cost):**
```
The most insidious part: none of this shows up in your metrics.

You shipped features. You merged PRs. You attended meetings.

But the deep work — the architectural thinking, the hard 
debugging, the creative solution — that requires unbroken 
focus blocks that AI-assisted workflows systematically prevent.

Velocity theater: the metrics look good, the understanding 
is deteriorating.
```

**Tweet 5 (What Actually Helps):**
```
Evidence-based recovery:

1. Batched AI windows: Use AI for 30 min, then 90 min 
   of pure implementation. Don't alternate.
2. Complete-then-review: Finish a feature yourself, 
   then ask AI to review it. Not the other way around.
3. One AI-free morning per week: Forces the brain to 
   work without the scaffold. Uncomfortable. Essential.
4. Transition rituals: A 5-min walk between AI work 
   and deep work. Sounds ridiculous. Works.
```
