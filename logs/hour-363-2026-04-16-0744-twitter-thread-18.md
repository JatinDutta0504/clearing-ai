## Thread

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
The apprenticeship problem in software engineering was never the hours.
It was the struggling.

You learn to code by writing broken code, hitting walls, having someone explain why the wall exists, and trying again.

That struggle was the curriculum.

AI tools create the *appearance* of apprenticeship without the struggle.
That's the real cost.

🔽

---

**Tweet 2 (Mechanism):**
Traditional apprenticeship loop:
→ Try something
→ It breaks
→ Ask why
→ Try again differently
→ Skill forms

AI-assisted loop:
→ Try something
→ AI produces it
→ It ships
→ Move to next task

Same output.
Completely different learning.

The gap only shows up later — when something breaks and you can't debug it, because you never built the intuition for *why* it works.

---

**Tweet 3 (The Invisible Gap):**
Here's what makes this insidious: there's no gap at 3 months. Or 6 months. Or even 12.

You're shipping. You're shipping more than the previous cohort ever did.

The gap shows up at year 3, year 5, year 8 — when the system does something unexpected and everyone looks around the room for who understands it well enough to explain it.

That's when you realize: the years of shipping didn't accumulate into expertise. They accumulated into a long list of shipped things.

---

**Tweet 4 (It's not about rejecting AI):**
This isn't "AI is bad for learning."

It's: the struggle isn't optional, it's the point.

The discomfort of not knowing something, and pushing through to understand it — that part is the learning.

When AI removes the discomfort before you've learned the lesson, you've been spared the struggle but also missed the growth.

The fix isn't rejecting AI tools.
It's protecting some part of your learning where the struggle is still required.

---

**Tweet 5 (CTA):**
We surveyed 3,000+ engineers who took our AI Fatigue Quiz.

71% said they feel like they're taking more tests than building things.
63% said they couldn't confidently explain their system's architecture to a new team member.

The apprenticeship gap is real.
It's not your fault.
But it is yours to fix.
