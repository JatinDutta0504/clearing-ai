THE SUNDAY NIGHT RECKONING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

There's a specific kind of Sunday that engineers know
but nobody names.

It starts around 5pm. You're not doing anything wrong.
You're just... aware. Aware that tomorrow you open your
laptop and there will be another 40 suggestions, another
30 autocompletions, another sprint worth of AI-generated
code waiting for your review.

And you realize: you don't want to do this.

Not because you're tired from the weekend. Not because
you hate your job. Because the work itself has started
to feel like reviewing someone else's to-do list.

This is a real thing. Engineers have been telling us
about it in the quiz — the Sunday night check-in
question ("Do you dread Monday more than usual?") is
one of the clearest signals for tier 3 and tier 4
AI fatigue.

Here's why the dread is real:

When you write code, you build something. You made
decisions, you saw the problem, you chose the tradeoffs.
The work belongs to you in a way that feels sustaining
even when it's hard.

When you review code you didn't write, you're doing
something different. You're approving. You're catching
mistakes. You're a quality gate, not a creator.

40+ hours a week as a quality gate, 0 hours as a creator.

The Sunday dread isn't about resting less. It's about
not building anything real.

What helps specifically for the Sunday night version:

→ Sunday Morning No-AI Block
  Nothing AI-assisted until noon on Monday. Build one
  small thing yourself — a script, a fix, a refactor.
  Not to prove a point. To remember what it feels like.

→ The Explanation Check
  Before bed Sunday, ask yourself: can I explain the
  three biggest technical decisions made this week
  without looking at the code? If not, that's information.
  That's the gap to close.

→ The Week-in-Three-Sentences Rule
  Every Friday, write three sentences about what you
  built this week. Not what shipped. What you built.
  The difference matters.

━━━━━

This week's data point:

From 3,000+ quiz takers, the Sunday night dread
correlates more strongly with tier 3-4 AI fatigue
than any single work-day metric. It's a lagging
indicator — by the time Sunday nights get bad,
something structural has shifted.
