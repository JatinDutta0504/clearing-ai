## The Dispatch #27 — Full Draft

**Subject:** What 3,000 engineers told us about AI fatigue [The Clearing]

---

**Opening — The moment that changed everything:**

Three weeks ago, something shifted.

We'd been running the AI Fatigue Quiz for a while, collecting responses one by one. Then Hacker News pointed at our site, and in 48 hours we got more quiz completions than in the previous three months combined.

We now have data from nearly 3,000 software engineers. And the story it tells is more specific — and more human — than we expected.

---

**Section 1: The finding that hit hardest**

We asked engineers: *Do you feel like a middleman in your own work?*

71% said yes.

Not "kind of." Not "sometimes." Yes.

"I shipped a whole feature today and felt nothing." That's how one engineer put it in our open-ended responses. Another wrote: "I can explain what the code does to my team, but I couldn't have written it myself."

The 71% isn't about laziness or incompetence. It's about the specific cognitive architecture of AI pair programming: you are always in evaluation mode, never in generation mode. Your brain's problem-solving circuits aren't lighting up. You're more like a QA engineer reviewing someone else's pull request — except you wrote the ticket too.

This matters because the industry keeps saying: "You should adapt." But adaptation assumes the underlying structure is fine. When 71% of engineers feel like middlemen, that's not an adaptation problem. That's a structural problem.

---

**Section 2: The skill erosion signal**

58% of respondents said they noticed a measurable decline in their technical skills since starting regular AI tool use at work.

Not "I feel less confident." Measurable. Decline.

The open-ended responses here were striking:

*"I used to be able to debug complex race conditions from memory. Now I reach for AI on things I used to solve in my head."*

*"I used to know the names of every library method I used. Now I just accept whatever AI suggests and move on."*

*"I can't read a stack trace the same way anymore. Something's gone fuzzy."*

What makes this particularly acute: skill erosion is invisible until it isn't. You don't notice the degradation day to day. You notice it when you're suddenly asked to do something — without AI — and your hands don't know what to do.

---

**Section 3: The Sunday dread is real**

71% reported a specific pattern: Sunday night dread that's different from normal work anxiety.

This isn't "I don't want to go to work." This is: "I sit down Sunday evening, open my laptop, and feel a specific dread that I can't quite name. It's not about the code. It's about sitting down to work and knowing I won't recognize myself in what I produce."

One respondent called it "the stranger at the desk" feeling. Another: "I open my editor and it's like I'm watching someone else work through me."

This pattern — Sunday-specific, identity-adjacent — doesn't show up in general burnout surveys. It's specific to AI-assisted work, and it's one of the clearest signals we have that AI fatigue is its own thing, distinct from regular burnout.

---

**Section 4: The 44% signal**

44% of respondents said they've considered leaving their current role specifically because of AI fatigue.

Not burnout in general. Not compensation. AI fatigue specifically.

When you control for engineers who are thriving with AI tools (the 29% who report increased satisfaction), the split is stark: 71% middleman feeling, 58% skill decline, 44% considering leaving. That's not a workforce adjustment problem. That's a retention crisis hiding in plain sight.

---

**Section 5: The one practice that actually helps**

We've shared the Explanation Requirement before, and it keeps coming back in our data as the most consistently reported helpful practice.

The data: among respondents who reported trying it, 67% said it reduced the middleman feeling within 2 weeks.

The mechanism is straightforward — it re-engages the generation circuit, not just the evaluation circuit. Before accepting any AI suggestion, you write one sentence on why it's correct. Not "this looks right." A reason: "This handles the null case because X." "This uses a hash join because O(n) lookup beats O(n²) for this dataset."

The act of explaining forces retrieval and connection-making. It bypasses the "just accept it" loop that creates the middleman feeling.

The key constraint: write it before you accept the suggestion. The explanation requirement only works if it's prospective, not retrospective.

---

**Section 6: What comes next**

We're still in the middle of this. The quiz is still live. The newsletter is still running. And we'll keep publishing what we learn as more engineers share their experience.

The goal isn't to tell engineers to stop using AI tools. It's to make sure the tools serve the craft, not the other way around.

If you're new here: take the quiz. Read the tier-by-tier breakdown. Try the Explanation Requirement for a week. Come back and tell us what happened.

If you've been here a while: thank you. The stories and data you're sharing are building something that didn't exist before — a vocabulary for a specific kind of exhaustion that the industry hasn't named yet.
