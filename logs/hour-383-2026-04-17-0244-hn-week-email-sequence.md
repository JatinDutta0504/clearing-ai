## Email 3: Day 3 — The Explanation Requirement Deep-Dive

**Send:** Monday morning (72h after HN) — "The Dispatch" cadence day
**Subject:** The one practice that actually works [The Clearing]
**From:** The Clearing <hello@clearing-ai.com>

*Variant: If HN thread didn't take off (skip Email 2), use this variant opening:*

---

Hi,

Three days ago, a lot of engineers showed up here from Hacker News. Here's what most of them had in common: they couldn't name the specific thing that was making them feel off.

They knew something was wrong. They just didn't have the vocabulary for it.

So let me give you the vocabulary.

**AI fatigue has three layers:**

1. **The cognitive load layer** — Your brain evolved to solve problems, not to evaluate pre-solved problems. When AI handles the generation step, you're stuck in permanent evaluation mode. Your working memory gets clogged evaluating suggestions you didn't generate.

2. **The skill erosion layer** — The learning loop runs through productive struggle. When AI removes the struggle, the loop breaks. Skills you used to maintain through effort start to atrophy. You don't notice until you suddenly can't do something you used to do cold.

3. **The identity layer** — Code is how programmers know who they are. When you're a middleman in your own work — shipping features you couldn't reproduce — the craft satisfaction that anchored your professional identity starts to dissolve.

Most burnout resources address layer 1 and call it done. AI fatigue goes deeper.

**The one practice that actually works across all three layers:**

**The Explanation Requirement.**

Before accepting any AI suggestion, write one sentence on why it's correct.

Not "this looks right." A reason: "This handles the null case because X." "This uses a hash join because the O(n) lookup beats O(n²) for this dataset." "This error handler covers the network timeout case."

The mechanism: it forces the retrieval circuit, not just the evaluation circuit. Your brain doesn't get to stay in passive-acceptance mode. You have to generate an explanation, which re-engages the same cognitive process that AI bypassed.

The key constraint: write it before you accept the suggestion. Retrospective explanations ("yeah that makes sense") don't work. The value is in the prospective search for a reason.

**67% of engineers** who tried it for 2+ weeks reported the middleman feeling lifting. (From our quiz data, n=~3,000)

Try it for a week. Set a reminder: "Explain before you accept." Notice what you learn about your own code.
