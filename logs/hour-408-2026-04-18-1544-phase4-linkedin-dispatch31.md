## Issue #31 — April 25, 2026

### The Architecture Problem Nobody Names

The most dangerous AI fatigue isn't for junior engineers.

It's for your best senior engineers — the ones who care most about craft, who used to raise the hard questions in design review, who noticed the edge cases before they became production incidents.

What's happening to them: they're making fewer architecture decisions.

Not because AI makes the decisions better. Because AI handles the implementation so the decision feels less urgent. The module gets scaffolded. The pattern gets suggested. The senior engineer reviews and approves, and somewhere in that loop, the practice of deciding slowly atrophies.

The result: after 12 months of AI-heavy workflows, the senior engineer's judgment is thinner than it was. Not because they got worse. Because the practice that maintained their judgment got replaced by AI's judgment, which is optimized for average, not best.

This is the hidden cost of shipping more with AI. And it shows up exactly where you can't afford it most: architecture.

---

### Why This Hits Senior Engineers Hardest

Three reasons this pattern is worse for seniors than juniors:

1. **The Expertise Reversal Effect:** The more expert you are, the more you learn by confronting novel problems. AI tends to smooth over the novel parts. Ironically, senior engineers needed that productive struggle more — not less.

2. **The Authority Trap:** When you have domain authority, people stop challenging your decisions. The AI doesn't challenge you either. The feedback loop that keeps senior engineers calibrated is gone.

3. **The Junior Visibility Problem:** Junior engineers' skills atrophy show up in code interviews and PR feedback. Senior engineers' judgment atrophy shows up in production — months or years later, after the decisions have compounded.
