## Thread (8 tweets)

**Tweet 1:**
You shipped 400 lines yesterday.
But you're not proud of them.

You used AI to get it done fast. The code works. Tests pass. PR approved.

And yet there's a quiet void underneath. Like you ate a meal but you're still hungry.

This is the hidden cost of AI-assisted engineering. 🧵

---

**Tweet 2:**
Here's what's actually happening:

AI gives you velocity. But it takes something else.

Not your job. Not your skills.

It takes the thing that made you a craftsperson in the first place — the gap between starting and finishing.

That gap is where pride lives.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Think about the projects you remember most.

The ones where you wrestled with a problem for days. The bug that took 6 hours. The architecture you stayed up thinking about.

You don't just remember the shipped product. You remember the *making*.

AI short-circuits the making.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The result is a new kind of exhaustion:

You're busy. You're shipping. You're moving fast.

But you're not *building* anything you can point to and say "I made that."

And without that feeling — without craft — work becomes just work.

---

**Tweet 5:**
Managers see velocity. They don't see the void.

"Your team shipped 2x more this quarter."

What they don't see: engineers going home feeling like they contributed to something, without feeling like they *built* something.

The productivity theater of AI.

---

**Tweet 6:**
This is why "just take a weekend off" doesn't fix AI fatigue.

The problem isn't too much work. It's not enough *making*.

You can rest all you want. But if you go back to being a middleman between an AI tool and a product — you'll still feel hollow.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The fix isn't using less AI.

It's being more intentional about what you build *from scratch* — even if it's slower.

One feature per week written entirely by you.
One debugging session with no AI.
One day where you touch nothing but your own code.

---

You're just a craftsperson who hasn't been making things.
