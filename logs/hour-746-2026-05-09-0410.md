The problem: that research was done before AI tools existed.

AI doesn't just interrupt you. It loads your cognitive bus while you're trying to rest.

**Tweet 3:**
What "resting" looks like with AI tools:

- 30 min lunch: still thinking about the AI refactor you need to review
- Weekend hike: 3am Sunday dread about Monday's Copilot suggestions
- Vacation: you brought your laptop "just in case"
- Dinner with friends: you're mentally rehearsing your standup update

The body is present. The cognitive load never dropped.

---

**Tweet 4:**
Traditional burnout: you need to recover FROM work
AI fatigue: you need to recover WHILE work never stops

These require different things.

Recovery from traditional burnout = rest
Recovery from AI fatigue = cognitive boundary-setting while still working

Most "recovery" advice only addresses the first one.

---

**Tweet 5:**
What actually helps:

1. Build a 90-min "cognitive cooldown" before you try to rest (no screens, no inputs)
2. Try a full AI-free weekend — not as punishment, as a diagnostic
3. Notice when you're "resting" but still mentally in your codebase
4. The goal isn't more rest. It's actual cognitive off-time.

Your 23-minute walk won't work if your brain never stopped processing the PR from yesterday.
