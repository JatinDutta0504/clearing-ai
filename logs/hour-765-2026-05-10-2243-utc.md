### COMMENT 5: r/ExperiencedDevs
**Post title context:** "Anyone else feel like their debugging intuition is getting worse?"
**My comment (copy-paste this):**

This is real. It's called Debugger's Drift — AI debugging tools bypass the slow, frustrating process of reading error messages and tracing execution that actually builds your intuition.

Here's what's happening: when you solve a problem with AI, you get the answer without the search. But the search is where pattern recognition develops. You're training yourself out of the "wait — I know this pattern" feeling.

Bainbridge's Ironies of Automation (1983) described this: the more reliable the automation, the worse the human operator becomes at the underlying task. It's not laziness. It's physics.

Three signals that confirm you have this:
1. You can't explain why a bug happened without the AI's output in front of you
2. You recognize errors only after seeing them in AI output, not before
3. Simple bugs take longer to find than they used to

What helps: The Explanation Requirement. Before you accept an AI debugging suggestion, write down: "The bug was caused by ___ because ___." If you can't complete both blanks from memory — that's the signal. That's your calibration data.

More at: clearing-ai.com/debugger-drift (it's made for exactly this)

### COMMENT 6: r/cscareerquestions
**Post title context:** "How do you know if you actually like software engineering or just liked the job market?"
**My comment (copy-paste this):**

This question hits different in the AI era — and I think the answer is actually harder to find now, not easier.

Here's why: AI has made it possible to "do software engineering" without doing the parts that used to tell you whether you liked it. If you've been using AI heavily and work feels easy but empty, you might be answering the wrong question.

Try this diagnostic — the Explanation Requirement: can you explain why the code you shipped last week was built the way it was, without looking at AI output? Not what the AI did. Why that approach over the alternatives?

If you can't — that's not a failure. That's information. It means the AI is translating for you, and you've lost the signal that used to tell you "this is mine, I built this, I understand this."

The engineers who feel this most acutely are often the best engineers. The ones who care enough to notice the difference.

More context: clearing-ai.com/developer-identity

### COMMENT 7: r/webdev
**Post title context:** "I've done 50 AI-assisted projects but I still can't build anything from scratch"
**My comment (copy-paste this):**

The Tutorial Paradox. You're not alone in this and it's not a character flaw.

AI gives you the outputs of learning without the learning. Finished 50 courses but can't build from scratch because each project gave you a solution without giving you the problem-solving process. The gap between "knowing the answer" and "knowing how to figure out the answer" never got bridged.

The fix is unglamorous but real: one project per month without AI. Not as punishment — as calibration. You'll feel the friction again. That friction is the learning.

The first two weeks will be slow and uncomfortable. That's normal. That's the point.

### COMMENT 8: r/qualityengineering
**Post title context:** "How do you maintain test quality when AI generates your test cases?"
**My comment (copy-paste this):**

QA engineer here — this is one of the most underdiscussed problems in AI-assisted development.

AI-generated tests feel comprehensive. They have high coverage numbers. But coverage is not the same as quality. AI tends to generate tests for what it knows how to represent in code — not for the edge cases that actually break in production.

The specific failure mode: AI generates tests that pass but miss the precondition or state dependency that only exists in production under load.

What helps:
1. Review AI test output for what it *doesn't* test, not just what it does
2. Write your own boundary condition tests separately
3. Track which bugs escaped to production — retroactively check if AI had the data to catch them

More at: clearing-ai.com/ai-code-review-fatigue — covers this specifically.

The code ships. Your name is on it. The tests pass.

But somewhere between the prompt and the merge, the authorship got... thin.

This is what we call the Middleman Problem at The Clearing.

You're not burnt out. You're not lazy. You're not an imposter.

You're a translator in a conversation happening through you.

The fix isn't rejecting AI. It's reclaiming the parts of the work that keep your skills sharp and your identity intact.

Three questions before you accept AI code:

1. Do I know why this approach was chosen over the alternatives?
2. Could I explain this to a junior engineer without mentioning AI?
3. When this breaks in production, will I know why?

If you can't answer all three — that's the signal.

**Tweet 1:**
Before AI: you knew roughly how hard a task was before you started it.

The estimate was usually wrong. That wrongness was the point.

You learned what "medium" felt like. What "hard" looked like. What "I have no idea" actually meant.

AI removed the wrongness. And with it, the calibration.

---

**Tweet 2:**
Here's what estimation used to teach you:

— That you don't always know what you don't know
— That "I need to dig into this" is a skill, not a blocker
— That "hard" is information, not failure
— That scope and difficulty are correlated but not perfectly

AI collapsed all of these signals into: "Sure, I can do that."

You stopped learning what hard actually feels like.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Gloria Mark's research: knowledge workers recover from interruptions in 23 minutes on average.

But that's not the right number for AI context switches.

The real number: you're not just switching tasks. You're outsourcing the estimation itself.

The task feels easy because someone (AI) is handling the hard parts. You're not developing the feel for difficulty. You're developing the feel for ease.

---

**Tweet 4:**
This hits senior engineers hardest.

Your expertise is largely a library of "I've seen this before" — pattern recognition built from thousands of estimation mistakes.

The mistakes taught you the edges. The edges are the expertise.

AI is collapsing the edges. You're becoming an expert in what AI does, not what the problem actually requires.

---

**Tweet 5:**
Before you accept an AI solution, ask yourself:

"Do I know why this approach was chosen over the alternatives?"

If the answer is "it worked" — you didn't learn anything.
If the answer involves tradeoffs you couldn't have named before — the AI actually made you a better engineer.
