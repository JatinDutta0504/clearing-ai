## Angle 2 — Burnout vs. AI Fatigue Distinction
**Trigger:** "This is just burnout renamed" / "wellness BS"
**Tone:** Clear, firm, educational

```
Worth distinguishing clearly: burnout is occupational exhaustion — it's about 
emotional depletion from workload, control, reward, community, fairness, values.

AI fatigue has a cognitive dimension that burnout doesn't fully cover:

1. Skill erosion (capability you had that you're losing)
2. Identity fracture (you're a builder who now curates AI outputs)
3. Attention architecture disruption (AI creates a specific switching pattern 
   that traditional burnout frameworks don't address)
4. The velocity trap (feeling productive while becoming less capable)

Overlap is real. Burnout and AI fatigue compound each other. But the mechanism 
is different, and the recovery tactics that work for one don't always work for 
the other.

Full breakdown with comparison table: 
https://clearing-ai.com/burnout-vs-fatigue.html

Not arguing AI is harmful. Arguing that how it changes cognition is specific 
enough to name and address specifically.
```

## Angle 3 — Juniors Are Most At Risk (Not Seniors)
**Trigger:** "this hits senior engineers hardest" / "experienced devs have the most to lose"
**Tone:** Counter-intuitive, evidence-based

```
Interesting — the data suggests the opposite: juniors may be most at risk.

Seniors have a deep foundation to lose. They can feel it eroding because they 
remember what they had. That's painful but visible.

Juniors who started with heavy AI use never built the foundation. They won't 
recognize the gap until they're mid-level and realize they can't debug, 
architect, or evaluate critically without AI scaffolding.

They're building confidence on AI output, not on their own capability. 
The illusion of competence becomes a crisis when AI isn't available or when 
they need to operate above the tool.

More detail on the junior-specific risk:
https://clearing-ai.com/junior-engineers.html

Seniors are grieving what they lost. Juniors may not yet know what they didn't gain.
```

## Angle 4 — The Structural Problem (Not Just "Take Breaks")
**Trigger:** "just take breaks" / "work less" / "set boundaries" advice
**Tone:** Respectful but firm, explains why surface solutions fail

```
"Take breaks" is necessary but insufficient — and framing it that way 
misunderstands the structural mechanism.

The problem isn't insufficient rest. It's the attention architecture AI creates:

Every context switch from deep work → AI tool → deep work costs 23 minutes 
of full re-engagement (Gloria Mark, UC Irvine).

A typical engineer switches 15-20 times per hour during an AI-assisted workday.

15 switches × 23 min = 5.75 hours of accumulated cognitive recovery debt per day.

You cannot rest your way out of a structural problem. You can't take enough 
breaks to recover from a workflow design that creates constant switching cost.

The solution is structural: batch AI use, protect 90-min no-AI blocks, 
reduce the number of switches per day, build scaffolding not solutions.

Practical framework: https://clearing-ai.com/ai-detox-plan.html
```
