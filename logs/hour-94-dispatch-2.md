Two weeks ago, 2,047 engineers took our AI Fatigue Quiz.

The most common score wasn't "burned out" or "fine."

## What the quiz actually measures

People assume it's about tiredness. That if you'd just rest
more, the feeling would pass.

But the dominant signal — 63% of takers — described something
more specific: a growing gap between the code that ships and
anything they could call *their work.*

That's not fatigue. That's agency erosion.

The craftsman relationship to code — the ownership, the stakes,
the "I built this and I know why it breaks" — that's what goes
quiet first. Before the tired mornings. Before the Sunday dread.

Nobody names it because the industry line is: *adapt or get left.*

---

## Three numbers worth sitting with

**71%** described their AI workflow as "taking a test" rather than
"building something."

**58%** noticed measurable skill decline — debugging, reading code,
explaining decisions — in the past 6 months.

**44%** are actively considering leaving engineering because of it.

The uncomfortable pattern: the engineers most worried about AI are
often the most technically serious. They've been at it long enough
to notice what's quietly not working anymore.

## The distinction that changes how you recover

This isn't about AI being bad.

This is about the specific ways human agency gets edited out of
the work — and what to do about it before the drift becomes
permanent.

Two concrete practices that help engineers rebuild the ownership loop:

**1. One small task, completely alone.**
Pick something beneath your skill level — a utility function,
a CSS fix, a config change. Build it without AI. Ship it without
AI. Notice what it feels like to remember you can.

**2. The Explanation Requirement.**
Before accepting any AI suggestion, say (or write) why it makes
sense. Not what it does — *why it's right.* The gap between
"AI said it" and "I understand why" is where the muscle lives.

**3. Batch your AI, protect your depth.**
Set a set of 2-hour windows per week where nothing AI touches
your work. Not for purity. For calibration. You can't spot AI's
mistakes if you've forgotten what correct looks like.
