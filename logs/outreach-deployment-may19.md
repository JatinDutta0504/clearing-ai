TWEET 1 (Hook):
Stack Overflow has a traffic problem.

Not the "AI is killing SO" narrative. Something deeper.

Engineers used to go there to learn.
Now they go there to copy-paste and leave.

That's not Stack Overflow's fault. That's a symptom of how we're being conditioned to use AI.

A thread on what we lost — and how to get it back:

---

TWEET 2:
Remember when you'd search a problem, land on a Stack Overflow answer, and read through the comments arguing about edge cases?

You'd learn something before you even solved your problem.

That "reading through" part is what built intuition. You were building mental models without realizing it.

---

TWEET 3:
Then something changed.

AI got good enough that you could get code without reading anything.
And AI doesn't have comments arguing about edge cases.
It just gives you the code.

You solve today's problem. But tomorrow's problem gets a little harder — because your mental model didn't grow.

---

TWEET 4:
This is the hidden tax on AI-assisted development:

The shortcut bypasses the struggle that would have made you a better engineer.

You ship the feature. You don't build the skill.

---

TWEET 5:
The uncomfortable truth: if you can't explain why the AI-generated code works — you didn't learn how to solve that class of problem.

You learned how to prompt.

Those are different skills. And one of them doesn't transfer.

---

TWEET 6:
I built an AI fatigue quiz for engineers.
Takes 90 seconds.
Helps you figure out where you actually are.

clearing-ai.com/quiz

---

TWEET 7:
The antidote isn't rejecting AI tools.

It's using them deliberately.

One rule that helps: if you can't explain why the AI's approach works — you're not done learning it. You just got past the problem.

---

TWEET 8:
What's the last thing you understood deeply because of how you learned it?

(Not "what did you get working" — what did you actually understand?)

---

TWEET 9:
This is why I keep building The Clearing.

Because "it works" and "I understand it" are diverging in our industry.
And most engineers don't realize it until the gap is huge.

Check where you stand: clearing-ai.com/quiz

---

TWEET 10:
Save this. Share it with the engineer who needs to hear it.

And if you're a senior engineer — ask the junior on your team: "do you understand why this works?" More than you'd expect, they'll want to know.
