## Thread (7 tweets)

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
You're a staff engineer with 10 years of experience.
You ship 3x faster than you did in 2019.
You're also more exhausted than you've ever been.

That's not burnout. That's the Senior Engineer Paradox.

---

**Tweet 2 (The paradox):**
Here's what happened:
2019: 8 hours to ship a feature. You understood every line.
2026: 2 hours to ship the same feature. The AI wrote 80% of it.

You got faster. You also got shallower.

Speed without depth = exhaustion without accomplishment.

---

**Tweet 3 (Why seniors feel it more):**
Junior engineers adapt quickly — they never had the "before."
Senior engineers remember what it felt like to own code completely.

The gap between "what you used to feel" and "what you feel now" is the size of your expertise.

And that gap is painful.

---

**Tweet 4 (The invisible deskilling):**
The hardest part: you can't see your own deskilling.

Your metrics look fine. Your PRs close. Your standups are confident.
But open a blank file at 11pm and try to build something from scratch.

The fear you feel there is real. It's not imposter syndrome.
It's the Competence Illusion — and it compounds for senior engineers.

---

**Tweet 5 (Why this matters now):**
At some point, you'll need to:
- Debug something AI didn't write
- Make an architectural decision without AI input
- Teach someone something you learned before AI existed
- Leave a job where AI is assumed

The skills you stop using are the skills you'll need most later.

---

**Tweet 6 (The reframe):**
The goal isn't to use less AI.
The goal is to stay the author of your own expertise.

That means:
- Regular no-AI coding sessions
- Building things you don't prompt for
- Keeping a private code journal
- Defining "I understand this" separately from "this works"
