Issue #7 — April 5, 2026

The one skill AI is quietly destroying (and how to get it back)

📅 April 5, 2026 ⏱ ~5 min read 👤 Sunny + The Clearing team ▼

Two weeks ago, a senior engineer told me something that stuck with me.

"I can build anything now. I ship more code than I ever have. But when something breaks at 2am — really breaks — I have no idea what's happening. I just prompt-storm until something sticks."

He has 12 years of experience. He is not alone.

This is what we're calling The Debugger's Drift — the slow erosion of the skill that separates engineers from code assemblers.

The paradox nobody talks about

AI makes you faster at writing code. It makes you slower at understanding it.

Every time AI handles a tricky bug, you learn that a tricky bug has a solution. But you don't learn how the solution was found. You learn the answer. You lose the diagnosis.

AI-generated code is optimistically written. It assumes the happy path. It doesn't know about the edge case your team discovered in production at 4pm on a Friday three months ago. So when something breaks, the AI's explanation is often plausible but incomplete.

And you can't tell the difference.

Why this hurts seniors most

There's a counterintuitive finding from cognitive science: the more expertise you have, the more you rely on context — years of patterns, failure modes, edge cases you've seen. AI doesn't just speed up your work. It strips away the contextual scaffolding that your expertise rests on.

The result: senior engineers often feel the drift more acutely than juniors. You know something's off. You can't quite name it.

The Explanation Requirement

Before you accept any AI suggestion — explain it out loud. In your own words. As if you're teaching it to a colleague.

"I fixed the null pointer by adding a guard clause because..."

"I refactored this loop because when the list is empty it would..."

If you can't explain it in a complete sentence — you're not done debugging. The AI bought you a plausible answer, not understanding.

The concrete test

Next time something breaks and AI gives you a fix: before you apply it, ask yourself — do I know why this fixes it?

If the answer is "it just does" — you're debugging with a blindfold on.

This week's most-shared story: the engineer who realized he couldn't remember the last time he had debugged something for more than 10 minutes without reaching for AI. His solution: one hour per week, no AI assistance.

Issue #6 — April 4, 2026

The Sunday Night Reckoning

📅 April 4, 2026 ⏱ ~5 min read 👤 Sunny + The Clearing team ▼

There's a specific kind of Sunday night that engineers know well.

It shows up around 9 PM. The week's work is done — technically. You shipped. You closed tickets. You responded to the review comments.

But something feels off.

You can't quite name it. You tell yourself it's the weekend ending. The Monday coming. The usual.

But it's not the weekend ending.

It's the specific feeling of having spent another week doing things you didn't fully understand. Solving problems you didn't actually solve. Shipping code you couldn't write from scratch.

You're not tired. You're hollow.

What AI Fatigue Actually Feels Like

Most content about AI fatigue focuses on productivity: "Are you using AI tools efficiently? Are you maintaining your skills?"

That's the wrong frame.

The real symptom is subtler. It shows up as:

  • Sunday dread that doesn't match the actual workload. The work was easy. The dread was real.
  • Relief when code works without knowing why. "It compiled. Ship it." followed by a vague sense of fraud.
  • Skills that used to be automatic. You used to know how to debug that. Now you prompt for it.
  • A gap between what you shipped and what you built. The code is yours but the work was done by someone else.

This isn't burnout. Burnout is from too much work. This is from the wrong kind of work — work that looks like productivity and feels like erasure.

One Practice That Helps

The engineers who recover fastest from AI fatigue share one habit:

Weekly "I built this without AI" sessions.

Once a week — same day, same time — they solve a real problem using only their own knowledge. No copilots. No ChatGPT. No code completion.

They don't do this to prove anything. They do it to remember what it feels like to actually build something.

The first session is usually uncomfortable. The second is a little easier. By the fourth, something starts to come back.

It's not about the skill. It's about the feeling. The feeling of "I made this."

The one question that tells you everything (answer honestly)

📅 April 6, 2026 ⏱ ~5 min read 👤 Sunny + The Clearing team ▼

You know the feeling.

It's Sunday evening. The weekend disappeared — not in a good way, but in that fog of recovery that never quite arrives. You're looking at Monday and feeling… nothing. Or worse: dread.

You tell yourself it's just weekends. Everyone feels this way sometimes.

But for you, it's been months. And the code you're shipping doesn't feel like yours anymore.

This is what 2,047 engineers told us when they took the AI Fatigue Quiz. Not burnout in the abstract — a specific, nameable erosion: the code ships, and you don't recognize yourself in it.

Today: the one question that cuts through all of it. Plus what actually helps.

The One Question

Before anything else — answer this honestly:

When was the last time you felt genuine pride in code you wrote?

Not "shipped." Not "merged." Not "met the deadline."
Wrote.

If the answer is more than three weeks ago, that's a signal. Not a failure — a signal.

These aren't weak signals. These are structural.

One Tactic That Actually Works

You've probably heard "take breaks." "Set boundaries." "Use AI mindfully."

Here's the one that consistently shows up in our data as actually working:

The Explanation Requirement

Before accepting any AI code as final, ask: Could I explain why this works to a junior engineer in 60 seconds?

Not "does it compile." Not "does it pass tests." Can you explain it?

If yes — you're still in the learning loop. The AI is a Socratic partner.

If no — you've exited the learning loop. The AI is a crutch.

Do this for two weeks. Track how often you can answer "yes." That number is your signal.

Issue #4 — March 24, 2026

The one tactic that actually works (and nobody's doing it)

📅 March 24, 2026 ⏱ ~4 min read 👤 Sunny + The Clearing team ▼

Week three.

If you've been following the 30-day plan, you're somewhere between "started to see the shape of the problem" and "slowly remembering why you got into this in the first place."

Today I want to talk about the one recovery tactic that shows up most consistently in our quiz data — across all four tiers, all experience levels, all stack types — as actually working. And almost nobody is doing it.

The Explanation Requirement

Here's the practice: Before you accept any AI-generated code as final, you have to be able to explain it — out loud, to no one in particular — as if you were teaching someone.

Not reading the code. Not glancing at the comments. Explaining it.

"So this function takes the user ID, then it queries the database to get their preferences, and then it merges those with the default settings because the query uses a LEFT JOIN..."

The act of explaining reveals gaps you weren't aware of. It forces you to confront whether you actually understand what the code does — not just that it works. It's uncomfortable. That's the point.

Why It Works (The Research)

Psychologists call this the retrieval practice effect. Robert Bjork's desirable difficulty research shows that the act of trying to retrieve information — not just re-reading it — dramatically improves long-term retention and understanding.

When you force yourself to explain code you didn't write, you're doing retrieval practice against code you should have written. The gap between what you can explain and what you can't is exactly the gap where learning used to happen.

AI didn't break your brain. It just made it very easy to skip the part where understanding gets built.

Two Weeks of The Explanation Requirement

Try it for two weeks. Every piece of AI code you accept, run it through this filter:

  • Can I explain what this does in 60 seconds?
  • Could I explain the trade-offs in this implementation?
  • Would I have solved it this way? Why or why not?

Track how often you can answer "yes" to all three. That number — week over week — is a real measurement of whether you're still in the learning loop.

Issue #3 — March 17, 2026

The Sunday scaries don't stop. Here's why.

📅 March 17, 2026 ⏱ ~5 min read 👤 Sunny + The Clearing team ▼

Something keeps showing up in the messages you send us:

"I took a weekend off. I felt fine. Monday morning, the dread came back like it never left."

That's not burnout. Or rather — it's not just burnout.

It's what we call AI Sunday. The specific flavor of exhaustion that comes from spending all week producing code you don't quite own, shipping features you're not sure you understand, and watching your skills quietly atrophy while your velocity metrics look healthy.

Rest doesn't fix it because rest isn't the problem. The problem is what the work has become.

Three things that actually help

1. One unhurried morning per week — no AI allowed.
Not a "no-code day." Just one morning where you build something the slow way, remember what it felt like before, and let that feeling inform the rest of your week. Two hours. No Copilot. No ChatGPT. Just you.

2. The Explanation Requirement.
Before you accept an AI's code as final, explain it — out loud, to no one — as if you were teaching someone. The act of explaining reveals gaps you weren't aware of. It's uncomfortable. That's the point.

3. Name the feeling before it compounds.
AI Sunday compounds because engineers think they're alone in it. You're not. 2,000+ engineers took our quiz and told us what they're carrying. Most of them felt exactly what you're feeling this Sunday night.

Welcome to The Clearing

📅 March 3, 2026 ⏱ ~4 min read 👤 Sunny + The Clearing team ▼

If you're reading this, something is wrong with the way you're working.

Not wrong like "bad engineer." Wrong like "something changed in the last 18 months that nobody warned you about."

You're shipping more than ever. Your velocity metrics have never looked better. And yet:

You can't quite remember the last time you wrote a function from scratch. You can't quite explain why that code you accepted last Tuesday actually works. You're learning less and producing more and feeling exactly none of the satisfaction that either of those things used to bring.

We call it AI fatigue. You're not burned out. You're not lazy. You're not "struggling with change." Something real is happening to the craft, and you deserve a name for it.
