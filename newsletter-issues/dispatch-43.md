# The Sunday Reckoning

*A letter about what happens to your brain on a Sunday when you've been running on AI fumes all week.*

---

If you're reading this on a Sunday evening, I want you to notice something: how does it feel?

Not "fine." Not "okay." I mean actually: what's the texture of your thinking right now? Is it sharp? Foggy? Racing? Shut down?

Most engineers we've talked to describe some version of the same thing: Sunday afternoon, the week ahead starts materializing in their head like a weather system forming. They can't quite stop thinking about work. They're not excited. They're not even particularly anxious in the traditional sense. There's just a low, persistent hum of dread — the sense that another week of managing AI, managing AI's outputs, and pretending everything is fine is about to start again.

This is not burnout. This is not imposter syndrome. This is the Sunday reckoning.

---

## Why Sunday Is Different

Saturday, you can usually recover. You're not thinking about work. You might do something fun. You might exercise. You might see people. Saturday still feels like your life.

Sunday is when the week's residue catches up with you.

Here's what happens: after five days of an AI-assisted workflow, your brain has been running in a very specific mode. You're processing AI outputs constantly. You're reviewing code you didn't write in the way you would have written it. You're making decisions about things AI surfaced but you don't fully remember deciding. You're producing at a pace that's artificially inflated by tools — and that pace has become the new expectation.

Your brain knows, somewhere below conscious thought, that you did not actually do all of that work in the way that counts. It knows you generated a lot. It knows the code exists. But it also knows — with a precision that conscious thought can't quite articulate — that the work doesn't have your fingerprints on it in the way that matters.

That's what the Sunday reckoning is. It's your brain running the math on a week of producing without owning.

---

## The Three Things That Make Sunday Worse When You're Using AI Heavily

**1. You can't point to what was hard.**

In a traditional week, Sunday often brought a certain kind of satisfaction: you knew what you'd done. The feature you shipped, the bug you wrestled with for two days, the architecture decision you made, the code review where you caught something important. You could feel the week as a series of real things that you did.

With AI, the week becomes a blur. Things got built. PRs got merged. A lot got shipped. But it's hard to find the moments that were specifically, distinctly yours. The AI was there for most of them, smoothing the path, filling in the gaps, generating the answer when you were three minutes from being stuck.

Sunday doesn't reward you with satisfaction for AI-assisted productivity. And your brain notices the absence.

**2. Monday already feels like it's going to be overwhelming.**

Traditional Sunday dread had a specific flavor: too many meetings, the difficult conversation, the hard problem. It was scoped.

AI-era Sunday dread is different. It's not about specific things. It's about a general sense that the week will be too much — too many pings, too many AI outputs to review, too many decisions to make without time to think, too many messages to catch up on. The overwhelm is ambient now, not specific.

This is the Sunday reckoning compounding across weeks. Each Sunday you're not recovering from one hard week. You're carrying forward the residue of five weeks of AI-assisted production without ownership.

**3. You can't explain it to anyone.**

This is the part that makes it isolating.

If you told your manager or your teammates: "I feel like I worked all week but I don't know what I actually did," they would look at you strangely. They can see your commit history. They can see the PRs you closed. They can see the output. In every visible metric, you were productive.

The experience you're having — the foggy, unattached, vaguely guilty sense that you didn't really work — has no name. It doesn't fit into the categories that people use at work. So you don't talk about it.

The Sunday reckoning happens in silence.

## A Framework That Helps: The AI Decision Stack

Here's one concrete thing you can do this week — not to eliminate the Sunday reckoning, but to change your relationship with it.

Every Friday afternoon, before you close your laptop, spend 10 minutes writing out what you call your **AI Decision Stack**: for each significant AI use during the week, what did AI do, what did you do, and why was your contribution necessary?

It looks like this:

```
Friday ritual: The AI Decision Stack
──────────────────────────────────────
For each significant AI-assisted task this week:

1. What did I ask AI to do?
   → [What you delegated]

2. What did I already know that made my ask better than random?
   → [Your context, constraints, domain knowledge]

3. What did AI get wrong, miss, or nearly mess up?
   → [What you caught and corrected]

4. What decision did I make after seeing the output?
   → [Your judgment applied to AI's suggestion]

5. What would have happened if I'd done this without AI?
   → [Your honest assessment of the trade-off]
```

This is not a self-congratulatory exercise. It's a calibration practice. The goal is to make visible the thinking that AI assistance makes invisible.

Over time, this changes how you experience AI use. Instead of AI-assisted work feeling like producing without owning, it starts to feel like what it actually is: leveraging a tool with judgment. Your judgment is the differentiator. The AI Decision Stack makes that legible to you.

## The Sunday Reckoning Self-Test

Try this on Sunday afternoon. Answer honestly:

**1. How would you describe this past week in terms of what you personally contributed?**
- I can clearly name my specific contributions
- I know I worked but I'm not sure what specifically was mine
- It feels like AI did most of it and I just managed the process
- I genuinely don't know how to answer this

**2. When you think about Monday, what comes first?**
- Specific tasks or meetings I need to handle
- A vague sense of too much to do and not enough time/clarity
- Dread about having to pretend I'm on top of things
- I'm not sure — I try not to think about it

**3. How's your sleep been this week?**
- Fine — 7-8 hours, waking up rested
- Okay — mostly sleeping but not feeling restored
- Poor — waking up tired, trouble falling asleep
- Bad — significant sleep problems all week

**4. How does it feel to think about the code you wrote this week?**
- Clear — I know what I wrote and why
- Somewhat clear — I can remember most of it
- Fuzzy — I remember it was built but not the details
- Like it belongs to someone else

**5. If someone asked you what you're proud of from this week, could you answer?**
- Yes, easily — I have specific things
- Sort of — I can think of a couple things
- Not really — nothing comes to mind
- It would feel hollow to try to answer this

**Scoring:** If you're answering 3 or 4 on most of these — you're not in crisis. You're in the Sunday reckoning zone. The AI Decision Stack, starting this Friday, is how you start to close the gap.

---

## What to Do This Week

Three things, in order of immediacy:

**Tonight:** Don't try to prep for Monday. Don't review your calendar. Don't catch up on messages. Close your laptop and do one thing that has nothing to do with technology — ideally something with your hands, or outdoors, or with another person.

**Friday afternoon:** Run your first AI Decision Stack. Ten minutes. Write it down. The act of naming your contribution is the intervention.

**Next week, consistently:** Start building a no-AI zone — even 30 minutes a day of coding without AI tools. This is the fastest way to recover your sense of ownership. It's uncomfortable at first. That's the point. The discomfort is your skill boundaries reasserting themselves.

---

## The Honest Framing

The Sunday reckoning is not a personal failure. It's a signal. Your brain is telling you something: that you've been producing without owning, generating without understanding, shipping without knowing. That's not sustainable for anyone who cares about their craft.

The good news: this is recoverable. The engineers who come out of this are the ones who started naming what was happening — first to themselves, then to their teams. The fog doesn't lift because you try harder. It lifts when you get your judgment back in the loop, visibly, consistently, on purpose.

Your Sunday evenings don't have to feel like this.
