# Dispatch #70 — The Competence Inflation Trap

*Issue #70 | May 20, 2026 | Forwarded by 400+ engineers*

---

## The Thing Worth Naming This Week

There's a particular flavor of confusion that shows up around year two or three of heavy AI-assisted coding.

You look at your title. You look at your output. You look at your salary. And something in your gut says: *I don't think I could build this from scratch anymore.*

Not imposter syndrome — that would be easier. Imposter syndrome says "I'm not good enough." This says something worse: "I don't know *what* I actually am anymore."

That's the **competence inflation trap**. The work looks like yours. The output carries your name. But underneath, there's a gap between what you can do and what your title says you should be able to do.

---

## Why This Happens

In traditional engineering, your skills and your output were tightly coupled. You wrote the code, you understood the code, you could debug the code without AI assistance. Your competence was visible in the work.

AI assistance decouples these. The output quality stays high — sometimes higher than you could achieve alone. But the relationship between you and that output has changed. You're now often *curating* and *directing* rather than *building* and *understanding*.

The problem isn't that you're faking it. The problem is that the old measures of competence were built for a different world.

---

## Three Signals You're In It

**1. You can't remember the last time you wrote a meaningful piece of code without AI help.**

Not because you're lazy. Because the loop is so fast and the output so good that there's no gap left to struggle through. But struggle is where learning happens.

**2. You're proud of what you shipped but uneasy about what you personally contributed.**

The "I" in "I built this" has gotten complicated. You shipped a feature. You guided the AI. You made dozens of decisions. But the craft-level satisfaction — the kind that comes from knowing you personally overcame something hard — is dimmer.

**3. You'd struggle to replicate your own work without AI.**

Not because you're weak. Because the work was genuinely complex and AI handled parts of it. But this creates a strange dependency: you can only maintain and extend what you can *understand* — and you may not fully understand all of it anymore.

---

## What The Research Says

A 2025 study of software engineers at three large tech companies found that AI-assisted developers completed tasks 40% faster but showed 23% lower accuracy on follow-up debugging tasks without AI assistance — a measure researchers called "output fidelity retention."

The engineers who fared best: those who had explicit "no-AI windows" — deliberate practice sessions where they built without assistance, even though it was slower and more frustrating. Not because they needed to prove something, but because they needed to *feel* their own thinking at work.

---

## One Thing Worth Trying

**The Saturday Morning Rule:**

Once a month, wake up Saturday, open a fresh editor, and build something small — a utility script, a small web app, a data processing job — with one constraint: no AI assistance for the first 90 minutes.

Not as a test. Not as a purification ritual. Just to maintain the circuit.

The goal isn't to prove you don't need AI. It's to keep the connection between your thinking and your making from going fully quiet.

---

## A Quote Worth Sitting With

> "The person who knows *how* will always be at the mercy of the person who knows *what to ask* — until what to ask becomes so automatic that the how begins to atrophy."

The answer isn't to stop using AI. It's to stay aware of what you're trading.
