## What 2,147 engineers told us

We ran a survey. The number that stuck with us most wasn't about skills or burnout.

**71% said they feel like middlemen in their own code.**

Not the person who writes it. Not the person who reviews it. The person in between — passing AI output from one window to another, formatting it, fixing it, shipping it, and never quite knowing if it was right.

This isn't imposter syndrome. Imposter syndrome says "I'm not good enough." This is something more specific: **"I can't verify my own work anymore."**

The verification loop — the one that used to confirm to you, every single day, that you were actually good at this — is gone. Replaced by a continuous handoff with a system that is always happy to help and never tells you when you've lost something.

---

## Why this matters more than burnout

Burnout is exhaustion. This is something else.

You can sleep eight hours, take weekends off, and still feel the specific anxiety of not trusting your own hands. The problem isn't how much you're working. It's that the work has stopped teaching you.

Every code review, every debugging session, every architectural decision — these used to be *learning events*. Moments where your brain built the model of how this system works. Now many of those events happen inside an AI's context window, and you walk away with output but not understanding.

This is recoverable. But it requires naming it first.

## One thing to do this week: The Explanation Requirement

Pick one AI-assisted task you completed this week. Don't look at the code. Close the laptop if you have to.

Now write down, in plain English, what you did and why. Not what the AI suggested — what *you* decided. Why that approach and not another. What edge cases you considered.

The gap between what you can explain and what the AI generated? That's your recovery territory. That's the muscle that hasn't been used.

Doing this once a week — just one task, just the honest version — rebuilds the loop that AI disrupted. You don't need to quit AI. You need to stop outsourcing the parts that used to teach you.
