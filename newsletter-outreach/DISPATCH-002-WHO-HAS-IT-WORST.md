## Who has it worst

We segmented the 2,147 quiz takers by role, seniority, and team size. Some patterns were expected. Others surprised us.

**The worst-affected group: engineers with 4–8 years of experience.**

Not the juniors (who never built the baseline to feel the loss acutely). Not the seniors with 15+ years (who often have enough perspective to name what's happening).

The mid-career engineers. They built their identity around a specific kind of craft. They learned to debug by struggling. They grew through the fog of complex systems. And now AI solves the problems they used to solve — sometimes better, always faster.

They're left with the question: *what is my contribution?*

The second-worst group: **engineers at companies with mandatory AI tool policies.** Teams where Copilot or Cursor or Claude is not optional. The adoption is forced, the skills atrophy is fastest, and the grief has no language because the company is actively celebrating the "productivity gains."

## The single schedule change most correlated with recovery

This came from reading through the open-ended responses in the survey data — thousands of engineers describing what actually helped.

The pattern that showed up most consistently:

**Blocking 90 minutes of uninterrupted, no-AI time every morning before standup.**

Not as a productivity hack. As a recovery practice. As a deliberate commitment to at least one decision per day that was fully yours.

The engineers who did this consistently described it as "recalibrating." Not producing. Recalibrating — the sense of touching your own thinking before the day takes over.

This isn't about rejecting AI. It's about making sure some part of your day belongs to you.

## Worth your time

**[The Explanation Requirement — The Clearing](https://clearing-ai.com/the-explanation-requirement.html)**

One mental model that actually works: before any AI-generated code goes into a PR, you have to be able to explain it. Not describe it. Explain it — why this approach and not another, what could go wrong, what edge cases it handles.

It's uncomfortable at first. That's the point. The discomfort is the signal. The gap between "I know the AI generated this" and "I could have gotten here myself" — that's where the recovery happens.
