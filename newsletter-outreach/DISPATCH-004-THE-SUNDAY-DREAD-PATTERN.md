## The Feeling

Sunday evening. You know what's coming.

Not the work itself — you can handle work. It's the specific weight of Monday morning: opening your laptop, seeing the backlog, remembering what you were supposed to have finished, and feeling the slow creep of anxiety that started sometime around 4 PM when you stopped pretending the weekend was real.

This feeling existed before AI. But engineers who talk to us about it describe something different now.

Pre-AI, Sunday dread was about volume. Too much to do, too little time.

After AI, Sunday dread is about something harder to name. The tasks are done. The PRs are merged. The week looked productive on paper. And yet there's a quiet horror at the thought of opening the code — not because of what you'll find, but because you're not sure you could explain what's in it.

The code shipped. You didn't learn anything. And next week, it starts again.

---

## What the Quiz Data Shows

We ask on the AI Fatigue Quiz: "When do you feel worst about your work?"

The top answer isn't Monday morning. It's Sunday evening.

Not because of deadline pressure. Because Sunday is when the reflection happens — when there's enough quiet to notice that another week passed and you couldn't quite locate where you were in it.

The second-most common answer: "During code review." Specifically, the moment when you're reviewing an AI-generated PR and you realize you could approve it with confidence but couldn't write it with confidence — and nobody can tell the difference.

---

## One Question to Sit With This Week

Before you close the laptop Sunday, ask yourself:

*Did I make any decisions this week that were entirely mine?*

Not "did AI help me decide" — AI is a tool, that's fine. The question is: did you have moments where the choice was yours, the reasoning was yours, and you could have gone a different direction?

If the answer is no — or if you're not sure — that's the signal. The work is still happening. The identity underneath it is getting quieter.

That's recoverable. But it requires noticing.

## Worth your time

**[The Explanation Requirement — The Clearing](https://clearing-ai.com/the-explanation-requirement.html)**

The practice that cuts through this: after any AI-assisted task, close the tab and explain in plain English what happened. Not what the AI said. What you understand now that you didn't before.

It sounds simple. It feels uncomfortable the first few times. That's the point.

## This week's reading

*The Manager's Path* — Camille Fournier

Not just for managers. For anyone who wants to understand the systems that shape engineering culture — and what those systems do to the people inside them. The chapter on "the optics of productivity" is worth the price of admission for engineers who feel like they're performing their work instead of doing it.
