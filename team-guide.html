For Managers

The Manager's Guide to AI Fatigue

How to recognize, name, and address AI fatigue on your engineering team.

What you might be seeing

Engineers who used to be energized are quieter in design reviews. Velocity looks fine on paper, but something feels off. PRs are bigger but you sense less thinking behind them. Someone who loved hard problems now seems to just want to close tickets.

This is AI fatigue — and it's not a performance issue. It's a cognitive load issue that the industry hasn't caught up to yet.

Conversation starters

In 1:1s, try these instead of the usual "how are things going?"

  • "What kind of work have you been enjoying lately? What's been draining?"
  • "Do you feel like you're learning things right now, or mostly executing?"
  • "How's the AI tooling working for you — helpful, neutral, or more stressful than it looks?"

You're not diagnosing. You're opening a door.

Structural changes that help

  • Protect deep work time. Block 2-hour focus windows. Make it a team norm to not have meetings before 10am twice a week.
  • Create AI-free problems. Assign small exploratory tasks where the expectation is handwritten reasoning — not AI output. Make this feel like a gift, not a test.
  • Normalize slower pace. If your velocity metrics have inflated because of AI, recalibrate. Faster output ≠ better engineering. Name that out loud.
  • Reduce the review burden. AI-generated code is often plausible-but-wrong. If engineers are spending 40% of their time reviewing AI output they didn't write, that's cognitive labor that doesn't show up in velocity metrics.

The thing nobody says out loud

A lot of engineers feel embarrassed to admit that AI tools are making them feel worse, not better. They think it means they're falling behind. Your job is to create space for the truth: that tool adoption has real costs, that adjustment takes time, and that how your engineers feel matters as much as what they ship.
