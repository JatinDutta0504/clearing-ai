## Tweet 1 (HOOK):
You shipped 47 features last quarter.

Your backlog is empty.

Your skills are emptier.

Nobody talks about this trade — the one where velocity hides the fact that you're running out of genuine learning opportunities.

---

## Tweet 2:
The empty backlog problem:

AI makes it trivially easy to clear work without building capacity.

You can always find something to build.
You can't always find somewhere to grow.

---

## Tweet 3:
Here's what happens to senior engineers after 18 months of AI-heavy work:

Their skills are stuck at the level they were when they started using AI heavily.

Not because they're lazy. Because the AI handled every problem right at the edge of their ability — which is exactly where growth happens.

The struggle that would have stretched them got outsourced.

---

## Tweet 4:
The scary part:

You can't always tell from the inside.

The work looks the same. Your confidence is stable. Your velocity is high.

But the floor is rising under you — and you might not notice until the gap between where you are and where your role requires you to be gets too wide to ignore.

---

## Tweet 5:
The signal nobody tracks:

How many times in the last month did you do something hard — genuinely hard, where you had to really think — and come out of it with a new mental model?

If the answer is zero, that's your empty backlog.

Not your calendar. Your learning.

---

## Tweet 6:
The engineers who handle AI tools well have one thing in common:

They protect the hard parts.

They deliberately seek out the work that AI would handle easily — not to prove a point, but to maintain the capacity they're delegating away.

---

## Tweet 7:
A useful test:

Take a problem you solved with AI this week.

Close the tab. Open a blank editor.

Try to solve it again, from scratch, without any AI help.

Notice two things:
- How long it takes
- How different your approach is from what AI generated

That gap is informative.

---

## Tweet 8:
The solution isn't to use AI less.

It's to notice which problems you're trading growth for velocity — and choose deliberately.

Every week: one problem without AI. Not to prove you're smart. To know where you actually are.
