**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
There's a pattern showing up in our survey of 2,147 engineers:

Before AI: you had a question → you thought about it → you decided → you acted.

After AI: you have a question → AI answers → you almost never think about it yourself.

You're not reviewing your own decisions anymore.

You're reviewing AI's.

---

**Tweet 2 (The Mechanism):**
Here's what's actually happening:

AI accelerates the decision loop.

Questions get answered faster. That feels productive.

But the friction you removed? That friction was where judgment lived.

You learned what to build by struggling through what not to build.

AI took the struggle. Now you're shipping things you can't explain.

---

**Tweet 3 (The Cost):**
The engineers feeling this most describe it as:

→ Can't estimate their own systems without AI
→ Can't debug their own code without AI  
→ Can't explain their own architecture decisions

They ship. They review. They approve.

They don't build.

The person who used to know things is now a middleman.

---

**Tweet 4 (Why It Spreads):**
The worst part: this is invisible.

You feel like you're working harder and understanding less.

The industry calls it "productivity."

The engineers calling it what it is: slow erosion of the thing that made them valuable.

Not burnout. Not imposter syndrome.

Middleman problem.

---

**Tweet 5 (The Fix):**
One rule that helps:

The Explanation Requirement.

Before accepting any AI suggestion, write one sentence explaining *why* it's correct.

If you can't explain it, you don't accept it.

This is the line between "uses AI" and "outsourced your judgment."

The engineers protecting their judgment: they're the ones who'll still be valuable in 5 years.
