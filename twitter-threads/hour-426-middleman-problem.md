**Tweet 1 (Hook):**
We surveyed 2,147 engineers.

71% said they often feel like a "middleman" between the AI tool and the actual output.

Not confidently shipping code. Just curating responses.

A thread on what it means and why it matters. 🧵

---

**Tweet 2 (The feeling):**
That middleman feeling has a specific texture:

You shipped the code. You take the code review comments. But when someone asks "how does this actually work?" — you realize you can't explain it without the AI layer.

It's not imposter syndrome.

It's functional change. You genuinely can't do what you used to do. The skill is eroding in real time.

---

**Tweet 3 (The shame problem):**
Here's why engineers don't talk about this:

The industry tells you: "Just adapt. Learn to use the tools. The good engineers use AI."

So when AI is making you feel like a fraud — you assume the problem is you.

But 71% feeling like a middleman isn't a personal failure.

It's a structural problem.

---

**Tweet 4 (The measurement):**
We built a quiz to measure where you are on the AI fatigue spectrum.

12 questions. 4 severity tiers.

No email required.

---

**Tweet 5 (The recovery insight):**
The engineers recovering fastest aren't the ones who use AI more carefully.

They're the ones who occasionally use NO AI — and rebuild the skill by teaching it back to themselves.

The "Explanation Requirement": before you accept an AI solution, explain it out loud as if teaching someone else. If you can't, the skill still matters.

---

**Tweet 6 (Why this matters for the industry):**
71% middleman feeling. 58% measurable skill erosion. 44% considering leaving.

These aren't soft numbers.

They're an engineering talent crisis in slow motion.

The engineers who built the infrastructure that AI runs on are questioning whether they can still code.

That's not a wellness problem. That's a knowledge management problem.
