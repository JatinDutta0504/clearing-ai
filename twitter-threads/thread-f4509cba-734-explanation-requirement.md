## TWEET 1 (Hook):
The quality of your AI prompt tells you how well you understand the problem.

If you can't explain it clearly enough for AI to help...
...that's not a prompt engineering problem.
That's a comprehension problem.

The struggle to articulate is the learning signal.

---

## TWEET 2:
Here's what usually happens:

1. You have a vague sense of what you want
2. You paste it into ChatGPT
3. You get something plausible-looking
4. You nod and move on

That "vague sense" was a gap in your understanding.
You just bypassed it instead of filling it.

---

## TWEET 3:
The engineers who learn the most from AI don't ask first.

They try to explain the problem to themselves first — out loud, on paper, without the tool.

Not because AI is bad.
Because the effort of explanation is where comprehension happens.

The prompt is a mirror, not just a command.

---

## TWEET 4:
This is the "Explanation Requirement" — a practice we built into The Clearing:

Before you ask AI anything, write down:
1. What you're trying to solve (in one sentence)
2. What you've already tried
3. What "solved" would actually look like

If you can't do step 1, you don't understand the problem yet.

---

## TWEET 5:
The test of whether you understand code:

Can you explain it to someone who has no context — without using AI to help you?

Not perfectly. Just clearly.

If the explanation comes out muddy, the comprehension isn't there yet.
That's data, not failure.
