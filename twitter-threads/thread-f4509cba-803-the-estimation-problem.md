## TWEET 1 (Hook):

Here's an estimation problem nobody talks about:

You estimate 3 days for a feature.
AI helps you build it in 1 day.
You feel fast.
You estimate the next one at 3 days.
Someone says "you did it in 1 day last time."

You can't explain why it was 1 day.
So you estimate 2.
It takes 3.
You feel like you're getting slower.

You're not. The AI's cognitive overhead just made the estimation invisible.

---

## TWEET 2 (The mechanism):

Traditional estimation: you know the work because you've done the work.
AI-assisted estimation: you know the output but not the work.

The work was: problem decomposition, pattern recognition, debugging, understanding failure modes, integration thinking.

The AI did most of the generation. You did the evaluation. Evaluation is invisible in the estimate.

But it takes real time. And you can't easily see where it went.

---

## TWEET 3 (The social pressure):

The uncomfortable dynamic:

You estimate 3 days.
Your AI-using colleague estimates 1 day.
Manager asks why you're 3x slower.

The honest answer: you're not slower. You're doing something different. But "I'm slower because I'm doing more cognitive evaluation work that doesn't show in the output" is not a politically viable explanation.

So you either: take the hit, use more AI to close the gap, or explain something nobody in the room has the framework to understand.

---

## TWEET 4 (The trap):

Engineers caught in this trap tend to do one of two things:

1. Use more AI to speed up, which erodes their own judgment speed, which requires even more AI
2. Estimate honestly and get labeled as slow, repeatedly

The middle path — transparent estimation that accounts for cognitive evaluation time — requires a level of psychological safety most teams don't have.

Most engineers quietly choose option 1. The trap closes slowly and then all at once.

---

## TWEET 5 (What actually helps):

The engineers who navigate this well are doing something specific:

They're maintaining a private log of actual time spent, broken down by: AI generation time vs. human evaluation time vs. integration time.

Over 4-6 weeks, the pattern becomes visible: AI reduces one type of time while expanding another. The estimate adjusts accordingly.

This is not about "proving you're working." It's about having real data on where the time goes.

---

## TWEET 6 (The reframe):

Stop estimating features. Start estimating cognitive contribution.

"How much of this problem do I understand well enough to evaluate AI's output?"

If the answer is "fully" — AI is a productivity multiplier.
If the answer is "partially" — the gap is your learning opportunity.
If the answer is "not really" — that's not an estimation problem. That's a knowledge gap.

The honest answer to "how long will this take" is usually "it depends on how much of it I already understand."

## ENGAGEMENT REPLIES (pre-written):

If "just tell your manager the truth":
> Most engineers don't have the vocabulary for this yet. "The cognitive evaluation overhead of AI-assisted work makes my estimates different from pure generation time" — that's not a sentence that lands in a sprint planning meeting. You need a simpler framework first.

If "I switched to story points":
> Story points don't solve this — they just hide the cognitive overhead in a different unit. The problem isn't the unit. It's that the work changed and the unit didn't.

If "just track your actual time":
> Yes — but for 4-6 weeks minimum. The pattern only shows up over time because AI's cognitive overhead is invisible in the moment.
