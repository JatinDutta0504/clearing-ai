## TWEET 1 (Hook — The Disappearing Act):

Here's what's happening to senior engineers right now:

They ship more than ever.
They review more than ever.
They mentor more than ever.

Their output is up.

Their expertise is quietly disappearing.

That's not a paradox. It's a process. And it's mostly invisible to the people living through it.

---

## TWEET 2 (The invisible transfer):

Senior engineers were never valuable because they wrote code.

They were valuable because they knew which code to write, which to reject, which question to ask before the problem was fully specified, which trade-off mattered and which didn't.

That judgment came from years of failures absorbed, patterns recognized, and systems understood at a level that couldn't be summarized in a docstring.

AI generates the code.

The judgment that evaluates whether the code is right — that still lives with the senior engineer.

But the practice of evaluating is how the judgment was maintained.

---

## TWEET 3 (The mechanism):

Every time a senior engineer says "yeah, that looks right" to AI-generated code, they're doing something that looks like approval but is actually: practice at accepting someone else's reasoning as a substitute for their own.

The approval takes 30 seconds.

The original reasoning would have taken 30 minutes.

The 30 minutes was the workout that kept the judgment sharp.

The 30 seconds is efficient. The efficiency is the cost.

---

## TWEET 4 (The devaluation nobody sees):

Here's the part that keeps senior engineers up at night:

When the codebase breaks in a way that AI can't explain, the question will be: "What do *you* think went wrong?"

And the honest answer — "I don't know, I'd have to dig in" — is a kind of confession that wasn't necessary two years ago.

Not because they forgot. Because the practice of knowing was quietly replaced by the practice of evaluating.

The expertise is still there.

The access to it is slower.

The gap is growing, and it's mostly invisible until it's not.

---

## TWEET 5 (The junior engineer mirror):

Junior engineers are getting a different version of the same problem.

They can't tell the difference between code they could have written and code they couldn't have.

They accept AI output as correct because they don't have the judgment to evaluate it yet — which is normal for their level — but also they don't have many opportunities to *build* that judgment because AI keeps bypassing the struggle that would have built it.

The senior engineer's judgment is atrophying from disuse.

The junior engineer's judgment is not developing because the struggle it requires keeps getting outsourced.

Different stages of the same problem.

---

## TWEET 6 (What AI actually evaluated for you):

When you accepted an AI-generated solution, what you didn't practice:

- The moment of not-knowing before the knowing
- The error you would have caught
- The trade-off you would have weighed
- The question you would have asked
- The "this doesn't feel right" that comes from deep system familiarity

All of that is real work that the AI absorbed so you didn't have to do it.

The work being absorbed is the judgment being built.

The absorption is invisible.

The loss of practice is invisible.

The gap shows up later.

---

## TWEET 7 (The framework):

Here's a diagnostic question worth asking quarterly:

"What do I know how to do that I could explain to a junior engineer — in enough detail that they could do it without AI?"

Not "what can you evaluate." Not "what does the AI help you with."

What can you still generate, teach, and be accountable for — without an external brain in the loop?

If that list is getting shorter every quarter, that's not burnout.

That's your expertise becoming invisible.

And expertise that isn't being practiced isn't being maintained.

---

## TWEET 8 (The reframe):

This isn't a case against AI.

It's a case for being deliberate about which expertise you're protecting.

The senior engineer who uses AI for code generation but debugs without it — who makes architectural decisions without AI — who does the hard reasoning work that AI *could* do but doesn't when the point is the workout — that engineer is making a deliberate choice.

The choice is: I am protecting the parts of my judgment that I cannot afford to lose.

That choice requires naming what you're protecting.

Most engineers never make it explicit.

## ENGAGEMENT REPLIES (pre-written):

If "I'm a senior engineer and I'm fine":
> The diagnostic question isn't how you feel today. It's whether your explanations to junior engineers have gotten shorter, vaguer, or more AI-dependent over the past 12 months. Ask a junior engineer. The answer is data.

If "this is just gatekeeping dressed up as concern":
> Not at all — the goal is deliberate practice, not AI rejection. The concern is when the practice stops without anyone noticing. You can use AI strategically AND protect the judgment work that keeps you sharp. Most engineers just never make that choice explicit.

If "my code quality is fine":
> Code quality and judgment quality are different things. AI-generated code often looks fine in a code review. The question is whether you'd know why it's right or wrong if the AI weren't there to explain it to you.
