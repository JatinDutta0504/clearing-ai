# Twitter Thread #74 — The Estimation Problem
# Hour 4263 — Fresh thread
# Theme: AI tools make it impossible to estimate accurately because you don't know what AI will solve vs what you'll solve

---

## Tweet 1 (Hook):
I used to estimate in hours.

"Two days." "Maybe a week." "Four points."

Then I started working with AI coding tools.

Now: I estimate in "some of this, some of what AI does, maybe."

---

## Tweet 2:
Here's the problem with AI-era estimation:

You don't know what you're building anymore.

Is this the hard part — the architectural decision, the edge case, the thing that requires judgment?

Or is this the part AI will solve in 30 seconds while you review the output?

You can't scope what you can't see.

---

## Tweet 3:
Traditional estimation: 
→ Break work into tasks
→ Estimate each task
→ Sum = timeline

AI-era estimation:
→ Task 1: AI does it in 10 min, you review for 2 hours
→ Task 2: You do it solo, 4 hours
→ Task 3: AI gets 80% there, you debug the edge cases, 3 hours

The sum tells you nothing.

---

## Tweet 4:
What engineers are doing instead:

"I'd say maybe two weeks, but honestly I'm just watching it work and hoping I understand the output well enough to ship it."

The honest estimate has become "I don't know." 

That's not a failure of skill. It's a structural change in what estimating even means.

---

## Tweet 5:
The uncomfortable part for tech leads and EMs:

If you can't estimate, you can't plan.
If you can't plan, you can't make reliable commitments.
If you can't make reliable commitments, the sprint planning ceremony becomes theater.

The velocity numbers still come out. The confidence in them just shouldn't.

---

## Tweet 6:
What helps:

1. Estimate in "AI time vs your time" — separate the tasks AI will handle from the ones that require your judgment
2. Add a review buffer — AI's output always needs your contextual evaluation, and that takes time proportional to how well you understood the problem
3. Track prediction accuracy — note what you thought AI would solve quickly vs what surprised you

The goal isn't accurate estimation. It's honest estimation.
