# Twitter Thread #75 — The Velocity Trap
# Hour 4263 — Fresh thread
# Theme: Team velocity metrics look great while individual engineers are quietly falling apart

---

## Tweet 1 (Hook):
Your sprint velocity is up.

Your best engineer is considering quitting.

These are not contradictory.

---

## Tweet 2:
Here's what AI-accelerated velocity hides:

The engineer shipping 3x as much code isn't 3x more capable. They're outsourcing 70% of the thinking to AI and validating the output.

That feels fast. It feels productive.

But somewhere underneath, they're quietly forgetting how to do the parts AI handles.

---

## Tweet 3:
The velocity metric sees: more story points, more shipped features, faster cycles.

The velocity metric doesn't see: the engineer who's stopped being able to explain their own code, who dreads 1:1s because they feel like a fraud, who has Sunday dread about Monday's standup.

---

## Tweet 4:
This is the management trap:

You see improved velocity numbers.
You think the team's getting more productive.
You plan based on that assumption.
The assumption is wrong.

Productivity theater, measured by the same metrics that create it.

---

## Tweet 5:
Signs your velocity is hiding a problem:

→ Shipping more, understanding less
→ The senior engineer's eyes have gone flat
→ Oncall incidents are rising — AI-assisted debugging creates confidence gaps
→ The "rockstar" is quietly looking at other jobs
→ Code review quality is dropping while review count is steady

---

## Tweet 6:
What this requires from managers:

The metric isn't "features shipped." It's "engineers who can still ship without AI, who feel ownership over their work, who aren't dreading Monday."

You can't manage what you don't measure.

Start measuring the things that don't show up in velocity.

---

## Tweet 7:
What actually helps:

1. Ask: "What's something you built this week that you fully understand?" — watch their face
2. Track "no-AI hours" — protect time where AI is genuinely offline
3. 1:1 conversations on actual state of skills, not project status
4. Watch for the flat eyes — it's a leading indicator, not a personality thing
