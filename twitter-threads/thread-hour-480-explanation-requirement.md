## Tweet 1 (Hook)
You've been shipping code for six months.

You can't explain why it works.

Not in the way where you'd know what to fix if it broke. In the way where you'd have to re-read it to understand it the way someone reads someone else's code.

---

## Tweet 2 (The Phenomenon)
The "Explanation Requirement" is gone.

Historically, if you wrote something, you could explain it. Not perfectly — but in the way a driver knows their route. You could look at a function, trace through it, and say "this is what happens here, this is why."

AI-generated code doesn't give you that.

You can verify it works. You can't verify you understand it.

---

## Tweet 3 (The Skill Erosion)
What quietly goes away when you stop being able to explain your own code:

→ Debugging without AI (you don't know what you don't know)
→ Reading unfamiliar codebases (you've always had AI for this)
→ Algorithmic reasoning (AI handles the hard parts)
→ Architectural decisions (AI suggests, you approve)
→ Teaching (you've stopped needing to articulate your knowledge)

---

## Tweet 4 (The Invisible Problem)
The scary part: nobody holds you accountable for this gap.

Code reviews pass. Tests pass. The ship goes out. You tell yourself "I could figure it out if I had to."

But you can't prove that anymore. You just have faith.

And faith is not a substitute for skill.

---

## Tweet 5 (The Junior Engineer Effect)
Something nobody talks about:

When a senior engineer uses AI and can't explain their code — what are the junior engineers around them learning?

That you don't have to understand your own work.

That "it works" is enough.

The mentorship transmission breaks at exactly the wrong moment.

---

## Tweet 6 (The Interview Problem)
Technical interviews test whether you can solve novel problems under pressure.

AI tools test whether you can use AI tools under pressure.

These are not the same test.

You can pass interviews while your actual engineering capability quietly atrophies. The gap only shows up when the AI is down, the context is unfamiliar, or the problem doesn't fit a pattern.

---

## Tweet 7 (The Way Back)
One practice that works:

Before you merge any AI-generated code — spend 5 minutes writing what the code does in plain English, as if explaining it to a teammate who has context but not this specific file.

If you can't write it: you don't understand it. Keep reading until you can.

This is not about avoiding AI. It's about making AI-assisted work actually accumulate as skill.
