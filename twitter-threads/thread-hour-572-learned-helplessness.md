## Tweet 1 (Hook):
The moment I realized I had a problem wasn't when AI wrote my code.

It was when I opened a blank editor and my first instinct was to close it and open ChatGPT.

That's not productivity. That's learned helplessness.

## Tweet 2 (Concept):
Learned helplessness: a psychological state where someone believes they can't do something, so they stop trying.

It usually shows up after repeated failure. You fail, you try again, you fail, you eventually conclude "this isn't for me."

Here's the AI version nobody talks about:

You try without AI. It's slow and frustrating. You reach for AI. It works. Next time, you reach for AI immediately — before you've even tried.

After enough cycles, you stop trying.

---

## Tweet 3 (Sign 1):
**Sign 1: You open AI before you open your editor.**

Your first action on a coding problem isn't "let me think" — it's "let me ask AI."

Not because you've decided AI is the best path. Because you've pre-concluded that trying first is pointless.

This is the automation equivalent of learned helplessness: the prompt comes before the try.

---

## Tweet 4 (Sign 2):
**Sign 2: You know you should do something by hand. You do it in AI instead.**

There's a voice in your head that says "you should really understand this yourself." The way you silence it: copy the concept into a prompt, read the explanation, feel like you learned it.

You didn't. But you can't quite name why it doesn't feel real yet.

---

## Tweet 5 (Sign 3):
**Sign 3: "I'll understand it later" becomes a permanent state.**

The Explanation Requirement was supposed to fix this: before you merge AI code, spend 5 minutes explaining what it does.

What actually happens: you paste the prompt again, read the answer, tell yourself "now I get it."

You don't. The explanation isn't the understanding. It's a substitute for it.

---

## Tweet 6 (Why it matters):
Here's why this is different from normal skill decay:

Normal skill decay: you get slower, you notice, you feel worse about yourself.

Learned helplessness: you don't even notice the gap anymore. You just... stop trying. And it feels fine. Until it doesn't.

---

## Tweet 7 (The path back):
The way out isn't "stop using AI."

It's simpler and harder: **deliberately do things AI could do faster, and struggle through the discomfort.**

Not as punishment. As rehabilitation.

Your brain needs the friction. The friction is the point.
