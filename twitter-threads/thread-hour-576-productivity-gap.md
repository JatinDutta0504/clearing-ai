**Tweet 1/9 🔥**
The weird thing about the last 2 years of AI-assisted coding:

I shipped more features than any other period in my career.
And I learned less than any other period in my career.

That's not a contradiction. That's the Productivity Gap.

---

**Tweet 2/9**
Let me explain what I mean.

Before AI: I'd struggle with a problem for 4 hours, hit a wall, Google for 30 minutes, have an aha moment, write the code, remember it forever.

Now: I open cursor, explain the problem, paste the code, move on.

Everything is smooth.
Nothing sticks.

---

**Tweet 3/9**
Here's what the research actually says about this:

71% of engineers report writing code manually less than before AI tools arrived.

58% say they understand code less deeply than they did 2 years ago.

44% say they'd struggle to build something without AI assistance.

The skills aren't just fading. They're not being built in the first place.

---

**Tweet 4/9**
You know what's wild?

Gloria Mark's research found it takes 23 minutes on average to fully recover your cognitive focus after an interruption.

We interrupt ourselves every 3-5 minutes with AI queries now.

We're basically running on a treadmill where the belt never stops. And we're calling it productivity.

---

**Tweet 5/9**
The signs you're in the Productivity Gap:

→ You can explain what code does but can't write it from scratch
→ You ship features weekly but couldn't tell someone how the system actually works
→ When AI is unavailable, you feel genuinely lost — not just inconvenienced
→ Your last "aha moment" was months ago, and you can't remember the one before that

---

**Tweet 6/9**
The uncomfortable part: everything looks fine from the outside.

The tests pass. The PRs merge. The features ship.

Your performance review probably looks good too.

The gap between looking productive and actually growing is invisible until it's not — and by then, you've lost more than you realize.

---

**Tweet 7/9**
I caught myself doing something last week:

I asked AI to explain a problem I'd been stuck on. Read the explanation. Nodded along. Felt that little "oh I get it" hit.

Then I realized: I could've solved it myself in 20 minutes if I'd just sat with the frustration.

The explanation wasn't understanding. It was a shortcut past the struggle. And I paid for it in actual learning.

---

**Tweet 8/9**
Here's my reframe — maybe the most important one I've found:

Every time you let AI do the hard part, you're not just outsourcing a task.
You're outsourcing the struggle that builds the skill.

The frustration you feel when you're stuck? That's not a bug. That's the learning signal.
