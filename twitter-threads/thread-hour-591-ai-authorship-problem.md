---

## Tweet 2:
Here's what "author" used to mean in software:

- You made the decisions
- You understood every line
- You could explain every choice
- If it broke, you knew why

AI is breaking all four of those things simultaneously.

Most engineers haven't felt the full weight of this yet.

---

## Tweet 3:
The uncomfortable part:

If you can't explain every line of your codebase...
you don't own the codebase.

You own a relationship with a tool that generates code.

That's a very different kind of professional standing.

---

## Tweet 4:
This isn't just an identity problem.

It's a liability problem.

When a production incident happens:
- You can't debug what you didn't write
- You can't explain the decisions to incident reviewers
- You can't predict edge cases in code you didn't design

Your AI generated code just became your professional liability.

---

## Tweet 5:
The legal dimension is still emerging:

- GitHub Copilot training data liability still contested
- Who owns AI-generated code? (US Copyright Office: human-authored portions only)
- If your AI-assisted code infringes — who's responsible?

Engineers are generating legal exposure they don't even know exists.

---

## Tweet 6:
What "real authorship" looks like going forward:

1. You write the architecture. AI fills in the implementation.
2. You review every AI suggestion — not just accept it
3. You can teach every decision to a junior dev
4. You can debug your own code at 2am without AI

If you can't do 2-4 without AI → you're not the author.
