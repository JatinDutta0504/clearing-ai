# Twitter Thread #49 — The Debugging Paradox
# Hour 626 — Fresh thread
# Theme: AI makes debugging faster but trains you out of the pattern recognition that makes you a good debugger

---

## Tweet 1 (Hook):
I used to be good at debugging.

Not fast. But good.

I'd sit with a nasty bug for 90 minutes. Trace the stack. Build a mental model. Find it.

Now: I paste the error into AI. Fix it in 30 seconds.

I'm faster. But I'm getting worse.

---

## Tweet 2:
Here's the uncomfortable truth about AI debugging:

The 30-minute session where you couldn't find the bug and had to really think about it?
That was the training.

The struggle wasn't a problem.
It was the practice.

---

## Tweet 3:
Good debugging requires something AI can't give you:

Pattern recognition from accumulated mental models.

You build those models by doing hard debugging sessions — where you have to hold the whole system in your head, feel where it "should" go, and notice when it doesn't.

AI skips that entirely.

---

## Tweet 4:
The Expertise Reversal Effect (Kalyuga et al.) explains why this hits senior engineers hardest:

What was once your most reliable skill — deep debugging intuition — is being replaced at the exact moment you relied on it most.

You didn't just lose a tool. You lost a form of professional intelligence.

---

## Tweet 5:
The metrics look fine though:

→ Mean time to resolution: down 40%
→ Bugs fixed per sprint: up
→ Debug satisfaction scores: stable

But the long-term signal is buried: your debugging intuition is depreciating in real time.

---

## Tweet 6:
What this looks like in practice:

Before AI: 2am production incident. You're in the codebase. You feel where the bug is before you find it.

After heavy AI debugging: Same incident. You start prompting. The AI is confident. You follow it. It was wrong about the root cause. You wasted 90 minutes.

Your intuition was right. You didn't trust it.

---

## Tweet 7:
The solution isn't to stop using AI for debugging.

It's to protect the practice:

→ Once per week: debug something real without AI. Not to prove a point. To calibrate.
→ When AI fixes something: ask "why was I going to solve it the way I was going to solve it?"
→ Keep a debugging journal. What patterns are you seeing? What did you learn?

The goal isn't to be slower. It's to stay sharp while being faster.
