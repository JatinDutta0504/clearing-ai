## THE THREAD

---

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**

The most disorienting thing about using AI coding tools:

You get better at using them.
And your skills seem to quietly disappear.

It's happening faster than you think.

🧵

---

**Tweet 2 (The asymmetry):**

AI coding tools accelerate one direction: output.

They decelerate another: skill growth.

You're shipping more.
You're learning less.

Same time. Same person. Same tools.

---

**Tweet 3 (The learning curve distortion):**

Before AI:
Learning a new skill took months of struggle → deep understanding.

After AI:
You can "do" the skill in weeks → feels like mastery.

It's not. It's access to the surface of a skill, not the skill itself.

---

**Tweet 4 (The valley AI skips):**

Learning has a valley:

You struggle → you fail → you recalibrate → you understand → you earn the skill.

AI takes you around the valley.

You never build what the valley was teaching.

---

**Tweet 5 (The compression problem):**

The old learning curve: 6-18 months to genuine competence in a domain.

The new curve: 4-8 weeks to "I can get AI to do this."

You hit "good enough" faster.
You stop before you hit "genuine."

---

**Tweet 6 (The decay curve):**

Here's the part nobody talks about:

Skill decay is faster than skill acquisition.

Unused knowledge: 2-3 weeks → first signs of erosion
Unused knowledge: 2-3 months → significant gaps
Unused knowledge: 6+ months → skills you genuinely can't access anymore

Now add AI. You're not using the skill. You're bypassing it.

The decay curve has steepened.

---

**Tweet 7 (The invisibility problem):**

The scary part:

Skill gaps are invisible when AI fills them.

You don't know what you can't do anymore.

The evidence is hidden by the tool that's hiding it.

---

**Tweet 8 (The distinction):**

Two very different states:

→ I know this and AI helps me do it faster
→ I know what to ask AI and I can't do it without asking

The second state is not the same as knowledge.

It's dependence.

---

**Tweet 9 (The skill preservation framework):**

Not anti-AI. Pro-capability.

Try this for one month:

→ One thing per week you build without AI
→ Keep a "learning log" — what did you learn that AI didn't teach you?
→ Monthly: attempt something you couldn't have done without your own skills

If nothing comes to mind — that's your answer.

---

**Tweet 10 (The reframe):**

The question isn't: "Should I use AI?"

The question is: "Am I still learning what I need to know?"

Output and capability are not the same thing.
Both matter. One is getting all the attention.

Don't let it.

→ clearing-ai.com/skill-atrophy
