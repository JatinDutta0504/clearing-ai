## THE THREAD

---

**Tweet 1 (Hook):**

I felt genuine grief when my AI coding tool changed its pricing model.

Not annoyance.

Grief.

And I couldn't explain it to non-engineers.

Here's why that reaction makes complete sense:

🧵 The Tool Loyalty Paradox:

---

**Tweet 2 (The context):**

You spent months building a workflow around this tool.

Keyboard shortcuts. Prompt patterns. Integration setups. The way it formatted its output so you could read it fast.

You optimized yourself around it.

It wasn't just a tool anymore.

It was a system you lived in.

---

**Tweet 3 (What happened):**

The tool changed. Or got deprecated. Or the model behind it switched.

And suddenly:

→ Your shortcuts broke
→ Your prompt templates stopped working
→ The output format shifted
→ The muscle memory was wrong

You felt less capable without anything actually happening to you.

---

**Tweet 4 (The uncomfortable question):**

Here's what I couldn't shake:

Was my grief about the tool?

Or about what the tool had replaced in me?

---

**Tweet 5 (The dependency pattern):**

It happened in layers:

1. You learned the tool's quirks
2. You built habits around those quirks
3. Your habits became your workflow
4. Your workflow became your identity with that problem space

When the tool changed, you lost not just a tool.

You lost the version of yourself that had mastered it.

---

**Tweet 6 (The comparison that's uncomfortable):**

Real craft loyalty: spending 3 months learning Vim, mourning it slightly when you switch editors.

Tool loyalty: mourning a cloud service's API behavior change.

The difference is: Vim trained you. The cloud service substituted for you.

---

**Tweet 7 (The skill loss you can't see):**

The dangerous part:

The tool was holding space for a skill you stopped building.

Every "I'll just use the tool for that" moment was a choice not to learn it yourself.

The grief isn't wrong. It's just pointing at the wrong thing.

---

**Tweet 8 (The practical reframe):**

Before you pick a new AI tool as a replacement:

Ask: what skill am I farming out here?

If the answer is "something I should probably know how to do" — that's your no-AI block for next week.

The tool shouldn't be your second brain.

It should be a way to offload the things your brain was never meant to hold anyway.

---

**Tweet 9 (The loyalty worth building):**

The tools worth being loyal to are the ones that make you better at the things you care about.

Not the ones that make the things you care about disappear.

If you feel dependent on a tool — that's worth examining.

Not to reject the tool. To understand what you're not building.
