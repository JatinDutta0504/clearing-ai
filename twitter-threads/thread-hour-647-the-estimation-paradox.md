# Twitter Thread #59 — The Estimation Paradox
# Hour 647 — Fresh thread
# Theme: AI makes estimation harder, not easier — the paradox at the heart of AI-era project planning.

---

## Tweet 1 (Hook):
Here's the estimation paradox in the AI era:

Before AI: You could estimate badly because you were the only variable.
After AI: You estimate worse — because now there are TWO variables you don't fully understand.

---

## Tweet 2:
The old problem:

Engineers underestimated because we were optimistic about our own speed, we forgot edge cases, we underweighted integration and debugging time.

We were wrong in predictable ways.

---

## Tweet 3:
The new problem:

Now your estimate has to account for:
- How long AI will take to solve it (fast, but wrong in specific ways)
- How long you'll spend reviewing/correcting/understanding the AI output
- The debugging cycle when the AI solution doesn't handle your actual edge case
- The ramp-up time for a new AI tool before it's fast

None of these are new — they're just harder to predict than before.

---

## Tweet 4:
What nobody talks about:

When you delegate the coding to AI, you also delegate the calibration.

You used to learn "this type of problem takes me 3 hours" by doing it 50 times. Now AI does it in 20 minutes. You feel fast. You have no data about what it actually took.

Your internal model of time is quietly becoming useless.

---

## Tweet 5:
The second-order effect:

You stop trusting your own estimates. So you add padding.

But padding for what? You don't know how bad AI will be on THIS problem.

So you add more padding.

And more.

The result: estimates that are simultaneously more conservative AND less accurate.

---

## Tweet 6:
A pattern that's emerging in high-performing teams:

They've stopped estimating in hours. They estimate in "sessions" — how many focused work blocks until it's done.

Not because they've solved the problem.

Because they've accepted that AI has made hour-counting unreliable, and they're optimizing for something they can actually control.

---

## Tweet 7:
What actually helps:

1. **Track AI time separately** — how long did the AI take, how long did correction take? Build a new dataset.
2. **Buffer for correctness, not just execution** — AI is fast at producing. It's unreliable at being right.
3. **Use historical AI performance** — if Copilot has been wrong about threading issues on this codebase before, add that buffer.
4. **Estimate the review, not just the code** — the code is almost free now. The understanding isn't.

---

## Tweet 8:
The uncomfortable truth:

AI has made the planning problem genuinely harder, not easier.

"AI writes code fast" looks like an estimation win. It isn't — it just shifts the uncertainty to a different part of the system that most teams haven't learned to account for yet.

---

The problem is real. The path through it is learning a new kind of calibration.
