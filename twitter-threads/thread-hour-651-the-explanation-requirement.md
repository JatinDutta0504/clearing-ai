## Thread Structure (10 tweets)

**Tweet 1:**
The most honest test of whether you actually learned something from AI:

After it gives you the code...

Can you explain why it works?

Not paraphrase. Explain.

If not — you archived it. You didn't learn it. 🧵

---

**Tweet 2:**
There's a specific cognitive phenomenon that explains why "understanding an explanation" is not the same as understanding:

It comes down to the difference between:
- Recognition (your brain passively accepts)
- Retrieval (your brain actively reconstructs)

AI feeds you recognition. Retrieval is what builds the model.

---

**Tweet 3:**
Here's what the science says:

When you retrieve information from memory, you strengthen both the memory AND your ability to access related information later.

When you just re-read or accept AI output, you get the recognition without the retrieval.

The result: you feel like you understand. But the understanding is thin — and it shows up when things go wrong.

---

**Tweet 4:**
The practical problem:

AI produces smooth, confident output.

Smooth + confident feels like "this makes sense."

But the brain processes "this makes sense when I read it" differently from "this makes sense because I built the model."

You can't tell the difference until something breaks.

---

**Tweet 5:**
The gap between what you can explain and what you can debug is the skill that actually matters.

When you hit an edge case AI didn't anticipate, you need the internal model — not the ability to prompt better.

The engineers who navigate AI well aren't the ones using it most.
They're the ones who caught their understanding before it atrophied.

---

**Tweet 6:**
The framework I use: The Explanation Requirement

After AI solves something for me, I stop and write one sentence:

"I added this because..."

Not what it does. Why it works the way it does.

If I can't complete that sentence — I don't move on until I can.

---

**Tweet 7:**
The uncomfortable part:

This feels like it slows you down. It does.

The velocity hit is real for the first 2-3 weeks.

Then two things happen:
1. Your base understanding stops degrading
2. The questions you ask AI get actually good

The slow part is the point. It's the learning loop re-forming.

---

**Tweet 8:**
Why this matters for teams, not just individual engineers:

If your team ships code where most of the "why" lives in AI systems — you have a bus factor of 1 for every codebase.

The next engineer to touch that code has no one to ask. They just ask AI again.

The "why" is infrastructure. It needs to live in someone's head.

---

**Tweet 9:**
The engineers who will thrive through AI disruption aren't the ones using it most.

They're the ones using it deliberately — with friction built in.

The friction is the feature.

It keeps the learning loop intact. It keeps the "why" in your head instead of in a prompt history.
