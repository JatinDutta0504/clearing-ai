# Twitter Thread #65 — The Calibration Drift
# Hour 674 — Fresh thread (May 3, 2026)
# Theme: AI makes your output look better than your understanding — the gap between "looks like I know it" and "I know it" quietly widens

---

## Tweet 1 (Hook):
There's a subtle way AI is quietly destroying your professional judgment:

You ship things you don't fully understand.
They work.
You take credit.

But your actual capability is diverging from your confidence in real time.

This is calibration drift. And it compounds.

---

## Tweet 2:
Calibration is the relationship between:
→ What you think you can do
→ What you actually can do

Good calibration means: when you say "I can do this," you usually can.
Bad calibration means: your confidence is being borrowed from AI outputs.

---

## Tweet 3:
Here's how it happens:

You use AI to build something complex. It works. You ship it.
Your mental model of what happened: "I built this."

But what actually happened: AI built the thing. You approved it.

These are not the same thing — even if the code looks identical.

---

## Tweet 4:
The Dunning-Kruger Connection:

Ironically, the more AI helps you, the less likely you are to notice the gap.
Because the output looks professional. It looks like you know what you're doing.

The gap between "I understand this" and "AI understands this, and I'm approving the output" is invisible to you while you're in it.

---

## Tweet 5:
Why this is especially dangerous for senior engineers:

Your calibration was built over years of consequence — bugs that taught you, incidents that recalibrated you, projects that failed in specific ways.

AI is removing the consequences that maintained your calibration.

You're becoming overconfident right as your actual skills are quietly eroding.

---

## Tweet 6:
The junior engineer version is different but equally dangerous:

New grads with AI can produce senior-level output on day one. Resumes look incredible. Interview performance is polished.

But the internal model — why it works, what could go wrong, when the approach breaks — that model has huge holes.

The output looks like expertise. The expertise isn't there yet.

---

## Tweet 7:
The scariest part:

There's no obvious moment where you notice this.

You don't feel your calibration drifting. It feels like you're shipping more than ever.

The first time it becomes visible is usually a production incident, a system you can't debug, or an interview where you can't explain your own code.

---

## Tweet 8:
How to check if you have calibration drift:

Ask yourself: "Could I explain why this works to another engineer without looking at the code?"

Not "what does this code do" — that's reading.
"Can I explain the why and the what-could-go-wrong?"

If that answer is slow, hazy, or requires looking at the code: there's your gap.
