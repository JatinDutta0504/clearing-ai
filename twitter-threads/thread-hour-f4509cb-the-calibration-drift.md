## TWEET 1 (Hook)

Your estimates used to be reliable.

Then AI became your primary coding partner.

Now: you think you know how long things take. You don't. Here's the Calibration Drift — and why it's dangerous: 🧵

---

## TWEET 2 (The Core Problem)

Pre-AI: You wrote code. You saw how long it actually took. Your mental model updated.

Post-AI: You prompt → AI generates → you review → it works. But "it works" and "you know how long it takes" are different things.

Your model stopped updating.

---

## TWEET 3 (Why AI Is Different)

With traditional tools: you make a mistake → you feel the friction → you adjust → next estimate gets slightly better.

With AI: you make a mistake → AI corrects it → you feel less friction → no adjustment signal → your model drifts silently.

The AI absorbs your mistakes. That sounds good. It also means you stop learning from them.

---

## TWEET 4 (The 6-Month Drift)

Here's what's actually happening:

Month 1: AI handles the hard parts, you handle the easy parts. Your speed seems faster. You lower estimates.
Month 2-3: AI starts doing more of the medium work too. You review more than you write. Speed stays high but understanding drops.
Month 4-5: You don't notice that your model of "X takes Y time" is based on increasingly different inputs than it used to be.
Month 6: Your estimates are now systematically wrong. Not overestimates. Wrong in a direction you can't see.

---

## TWEET 5 (The Specific Failure Mode)

Old world: "This feature will take 3 days"
→ You knew the codebase
→ You knew your own speed
→ Uncertainty was bounded

New world: "This feature will take 3 days"
→ AI generated 400 lines in 20 minutes
→ You spent 2 days reviewing, understanding, and debugging the edge cases
→ The 20-minute generation became a 3-day project
→ But you still think of it as "3 days"

---

## TWEET 6 (The Invisible Mismatch)

Here's the part nobody talks about:

When you accept AI code you don't fully understand, you're not just "not learning" — you're actively building a false model of your own abilities.

You shipped a thing. You think you know how to build that thing. You don't.

The gap between "shipped a feature" and "can build that feature from scratch" is now larger than it's ever been. And you can't see it.

---

## TWEET 7 (The Spot-Check Method)

How to detect calibration drift in yourself:

Once a month, try to build something small — genuinely small — without AI.

Not as a test. As a calibration.

If it takes you 3× longer than it "should" in your mental model, that's your drift measurement. That's how much your model has drifted.

---

## TWEET 8 (The Correction Practice)

The engineers who maintain accurate estimates in the AI era are the ones who regularly rebuild things without AI — not to prove they can, but to recalibrate their model.

Quarterly: pick one significant feature you've built recently. Rebuild it from scratch in 2 hours, no AI.

The gap between what you expected and what happened is your calibration error.

---

## TWEET 9 (Why This Matters More Than You Think)

Calibration drift doesn't just affect your estimates.

It affects your sense of what's hard, what's valuable, what you understand, and what you're proud of.

When your internal model of your own abilities drifts, everything downstream drifts: your confidence, your architectural judgment, your ability to mentor, your career clarity.

---

## TWEET 10 (CTA)

The path back isn't "use less AI." It's "build calibration check-points into your work."

Every significant thing you build with AI: can you still explain it without the AI present?

If not, that's your signal.
