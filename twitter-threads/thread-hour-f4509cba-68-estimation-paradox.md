# Twitter Thread #68 — The Estimation Paradox
# Hour F4509cba — Fresh thread (May 4, 2026)
# Theme: AI coding tools have broken the fundamental skill of estimation — because the work keeps changing

---

## TWEET 1 (Hook):
The first thing AI broke wasn't your code quality.

It was your ability to estimate how long things take.

---

## TWEET 2 (The Setup):
Think about what estimation used to require:

You knew what you could do in a day. You'd built up a feel for the work. You'd been wrong enough times to calibrate.

Now: you estimate a task. AI makes it 3x faster than you expected. Next task, you estimate it — and AI makes it 10x faster. Next one, 2x slower (because the AI can't do that part and keeps hallucinating).

Your calibration is completely broken.

---

## TWEET 3 (The Core Problem):
Here's why estimation is now almost impossible:

Before AI: The work had a stable shape. You could see the path from problem to solution.

After AI: The path exists, but AI walks part of it on your behalf — in ways you can't predict or measure. Some parts become trivial. Others become more complex. You lose the ability to see the whole path.

Your estimate is based on your intuition. Your intuition is based on experience. AI changes the experience every week.

---

## TWEET 4 (The Manager View):
Managers see this as "engineers not being reliable."

Engineers see this as "I literally cannot predict what AI will do to this task."

Both are right. And this gap is where trust erodes.

The engineer who estimated honestly before AI is now underestimating their AI-assisted output → looks slow.
The engineer who estimates including AI assistance is now overestimating → misses deadlines.

Both are wrong. Neither is lying.

---

## TWEET 5 (The Compounding Problem):
You know what happens when you miss estimates?

You lose credibility. You get watched more closely. People ask for more detail in your estimates. You add padding.

But padding doesn't help when the variable is AI — and AI is inconsistent across different types of work.

So you pad more. You sound less confident. Your estimates start to feel arbitrary.

The uncomfortable truth: the engineering discipline of honest estimation has become nearly impossible to practice when the work keeps changing shape.

---

## TWEET 6 (The Hidden Cost):
There's a secondary cost nobody talks about:

When you can't estimate accurately, you start to distrust yourself. You question your competence. You wonder if you're falling behind.

"You're getting slower." — but you're not. The ground just moved.

---

## TWEET 7 (What Actually Helps):
Engineers who've navigated this well have adapted in a few ways:

→ Track "time with AI" vs "time without AI" separately — it gives you a real baseline
→ Estimate in ranges, not points — "1-3 days, depending on how much AI can handle"
→ When an estimate is wrong, note what made it wrong — build a new mental model
→ Have explicit conversations with managers: "This estimate includes AI. The uncertainty is higher than before."

The key is honesty about the uncertainty, not precision that doesn't exist.

---

## TWEET 8 (The Framework):
Try this for 2 weeks:

After every AI-assisted task, log: What did you expect? What actually happened? Where did AI make it faster? Where did it make it slower?

After 2 weeks, you'll have real data about your AI-assisted pace. That's a real estimate you can trust.

Blind estimation is what's breaking everyone. Not your skill. The model.
