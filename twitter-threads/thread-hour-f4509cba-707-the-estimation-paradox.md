---

## TWEET 2 (The Paradox)

AI coding tools: "This will take 2 hours"

You: "Okay, that tracks"

30 minutes in: the AI hits a edge case it can't handle

You: "Let me ask it differently"

45 more minutes: you realize the AI's approach won't work at scale

You: "Let me just redo this properly"

Total time: 4 hours.

AI's estimate: 2 hours.

Your old estimate without AI: 3 hours.

---

## TWEET 3 (Why This Happens)

AI collapses the visible struggle.

When you estimate without AI, you include: false starts, debugging, research dead ends, edge cases you discover mid-build.

These are features — they're the actual work becoming visible in the estimate.

AI hides them. The estimate looks clean. The reality is not.

---

## TWEET 4 (The Knowledge Problem)

Here's the deeper issue:

Good estimation requires knowing what you don't know.

With AI, you stop running into the gaps. AI fills them. You stop learning where your knowledge ends.

When you hit an estimate-accurate problem, it's because you've seen that problem before — without AI, where you hit the wall was the learning.

Now the wall is invisible. The learning is gone. The estimate is wrong.

---

## TWEET 5 (What Good Teams Do)

The engineers and leads who've fixed this:

→ Add a "AI edge case buffer" — multiply AI estimates by 1.7 for anything involving novel integration
→ Track actual vs. AI-predicted time — build a team-specific correction factor
→ Count the "re-explaining to AI" time — it's real work, it's not in the estimate
→ Have explicit "AI hit a wall" conversations in standups — naming the pattern normalizes it

---

## TWEET 6 (The Deeper Problem)

When your estimates are AI-inflated, planning breaks.

Sprint planning. Roadmapping. Hiring decisions. Architecture choices.

All of them rest on "how long does this take."

If AI is making estimates systematically optimistic, the whole planning layer is compromised.

You're not in control of your roadmap. You're in control of a roadmap built on AI's false confidence.

---

## TWEET 7 (For Tech Leads)

The question worth asking your team:

"When AI estimated wrong, what actually happened?"

Not to blame anyone. To build a correction factor.

Because here's what's true: the teams that understand their AI estimation error are making better plans than teams that don't.

The error is learnable. The correction factor is specific to your codebase, your team, your architecture.

If you're not tracking it, you're flying blind.
