## Thread: "The Review Trap"

**Hook tweet (Tweet 1):**
---
You shipped 47 pull requests last sprint.

You reviewed code you didn't write. You explained decisions you hadn't made. You attended standups where you were more spectator than engineer.

And you're still exhausted on Friday afternoon.

This is the Review Trap — and it's quietly burning out senior engineers more than any AI tool.

🧵 [15-tweet thread]
---

**Tweet 2:**
What's happening?

Most senior engineers in 2026 aren't doing "real" engineering work most of the time.

They're doing:
→ Code review (1-3 hrs/day)
→ Architecture discussion (1-2 hrs/day)
→ PR explanation (1-2 hrs/day)
→ Meeting coordination (1-2 hrs/day)
→ Mentorship (1 hr/day)

That's 5-11 hours of WORK that isn't writing code.

**Tweet 3:**
The cruelest part?

This review-heavy work looks like "senior" from the outside.
But it feels like being a coordinator, not an engineer.

You spend your day in other people's codebases.
You make decisions by committee.
You ship features you don't fully own.

The job description said "individual contributor." The job became something else.

**Tweet 4:**
Here's the identity trap:

Senior engineers define themselves by depth — the hardearned ability to understand systems deeply, make architectural decisions, write the tricky code.

But the job now rewards breadth and coordination — being available, responsive, and facilitating other people's work.

You get paid for depth. You actually do breadth.

**Tweet 5:**
And then AI walks in.

AI coding tools make it easy for juniors and mid-level engineers to generate code fast.

So your review queue doubles. And triples.

Every PR comes with: "I asked AI and it gave me this, can you review it?"

You're now the quality assurance layer for AI-generated work.

**Tweet 6:**
The problem isn't that review is bad.

Good code review is essential. It transfers knowledge, catches bugs, maintains standards, and builds team cohesion.

The problem is that review has replaced — not supplemented — the deep, creative, architectural work that made senior engineering satisfying.

You're a review engineer now.

**Tweet 7:**
The compensation trap:

You were promoted to senior because you wrote great code.
Your growth path led to more code responsibility.
Your actual job now is mostly reviewing other people's AI-generated code.

You got the promotion. The work you loved moved further away.

**Tweet 8:**
This isn't anyone's fault. It's structural.

As engineers become more productive with AI tools, the bottleneck shifts from "writing code" to "understanding code."

That bottleneck is YOU — the senior engineer reviewing and explaining and maintaining context.

The more productive the team, the more review work lands on you.

**Tweet 9:**
Signs you're in the Review Trap:

→ You can't remember the last time you wrote significant code from scratch
→ Your deep work hours are consumed by review and meetings
→ PR explanations take longer than PR implementation
→ You're learning less on the job than you did 3 years ago
→ "Let me take another look at this" has become your default mode

**Tweet 10:**
The burnout mechanism:

Review work is high cognitive load + low craft satisfaction.
You're processing complex ideas, making judgments, catching subtle bugs — but you don't get the dopamine hit of building something.

The work is draining. The reward is absent.

This is the combination that produces exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix.

**Tweet 11:**
What separates the Review Trap from normal senior work?

Normal senior work = deep IC work + some review + some mentorship + some architecture

Review Trap = review + meetings + coordination dominate your day
The deep IC work never comes. Or it comes at 10pm, when you're already depleted.

**Tweet 12:**
The dangerous normalization:

"If I'm not coding, I'm still doing senior engineering work."

This is true — for one or two days a week.

If it's true for four or five days a week, you've entered the Review Trap.

The trap is comfortable. It looks productive. It feels like seniority.

It isn't.

**Tweet 13:**
How to reclaim the work:

1. Track your week in 30-min blocks
   If review + meetings > 60% of your time, the trap has you

2. Protect 2-3 "review-free" hours weekly
   Blocks where you build from scratch. Not review. Not explain. Build.

3. Be explicit with your manager about what you're NOT doing
   "I'm reducing PR review load to focus on the distributed systems redesign"

4. Push back on AI-generated PRs
   If a PR is 80% AI-generated with no explanation, return it with questions

5. Redefine your 1:1s around your work, not the team's work

**Tweet 14:**
The deeper shift:

Senior engineers need to renegotiate what "senior" means in an AI-augmented team.

It's not about writing more code than everyone else.
It's not about reviewing more PRs.

It's about maintaining the depth — the architectural judgment, the systems thinking, the hard-won expertise — that makes you irreplaceable.

That requires time. You have to protect it like it's your job. Because it is.

**Tweet 15:**
The question I keep asking:

Would you hire the version of you that's doing today's job?

Or would you hire someone to do the coordination work so you can do the engineering?

That answer tells you something.
